## Priya Iyer — Lead Instrumentation Engineer

**Provenance:** Returning character. Introduced in Ch1 (King-style revision; see `chore/pao-king-style-ch01-aanya`) as the construction PM who lost the Pune hospital bid at 4:47 PM when her SaaS platform and its single-sign-on dependency went down simultaneously with thirteen minutes left to submit. **Name LOCKED:** Priya Iyer.

**Identity:** Female. Indian (Tamil Brahmin family name; Pune-based at time of Ch1 incident). Age at Ch1: ~32–36; age at MERIDIAN-7: ~38–44.

**Backstory:** In Ch1 her hospital expansion bid (243 line items, six weeks of work) was lost when the SaaS construction-management platform stopped responding at 4:47 PM. The bid was won by a competitor whose platform happened to run on a different vendor whose dependencies had not failed that afternoon. Between Ch1 and The Crossing, she pivoted from construction project management to designing modular field instrumentation for environments where cloud connectivity cannot be assumed. She designed the Nansen ice-shelf monitoring instruments. Recruited because the instrumentation she designed will be installed by the crew during the dive.

**Tic / King-required residual scarring:** She still keeps three browser tabs open as a working habit (established in Ch1's revised passage). On the boat this manifests as over-testing instrument calibration — running the calibration suite three times when procedure requires once. The Director observes the tic; does not comment in dialogue but writes about it in the staff history. The tic does not heal in the chapter. Her competence still costs her.

**Role in chapter:** Brief presence in Act I (introduced), Act II (instrument calibration during transit, where the three-times tic manifests), Act V (deployment of instruments at destination). Does not feature in Act III death scene or Act IV silent-window scene.

**Identity:** Male. **Japanese** (Tokyo; NIPR-affiliated). Age 50–57. **LOCKED:** Dr. Hiroshi Nakamura. Senior glaciologist with thirty years of Antarctic research going back to graduate years at Showa Station in the 1990s.

**Affiliation:** **NIPR (National Institute of Polar Research, Tokyo)** — the real Japanese national polar research body, founded 1973, operates Showa Station and a network of Antarctic field sites under the Japanese Antarctic Research Expedition (JARE) program (running continuously since 1956).

Reports to Dr. Anna Yusupova for command questions, leads scientific decision-making for the mission. Bilingual Japanese / English (fluent technical English from decades of international polar collaboration; Japanese senior scientists at NIPR are routinely English-fluent).

**Personality:** Calm. Deliberate. The kind of senior researcher whose quiet presence shapes a watch. The "calmest man on the manifest" framing transfers cleanly from the previous French version — Japanese senior-scientist register supports it as well as French does.

**Role in chapter:** Background presence; one or two scenes. Provides scientific framing in the Director's narration when the mission's instrumentation deployment proceeds in Act V. ### Suppressed-artist register (CO directive 2026-05-06) — manga

Hiroshi was a teenage manga artist. Late 1970s–early 1980s, in the years when Tezuka Osamu was still actively defining the medium and Otomo Katsuhiro was just starting to publish what would become *Akira*. Hiroshi was good. He drew in the margins of his school notebooks; he submitted to amateur contests; he had the panel-instinct — the way to break a single moment across three frames so the reader's eye reads the silence in the middle. In a different family he might have apprenticed to a working *mangaka*. His was not a different family. His parents (mother a primary-school teacher, father a Tokyo-Metropolitan-Government clerk; PAO recommendation, can be amended) redirected him toward the gaokao-equivalent university-entrance discipline that produced the glaciologist he became. He stopped drawing seriously at university entrance exam time. He has not drawn a finished panel in forty years.

He still has the eye. **The chief-scientist register IS the suppressed-mangaka register made operational** — what manga teaches is to read faces, to register the silence between frames, to know what a person is not saying in the way they hold their hands at a console. Hiroshi watches; Hiroshi does not narrate; Hiroshi is the calmest man on the manifest because his thirty-five years of glaciological observation are layered on top of an earlier discipline of narrative observation that he set down but did not lose. The chapter where he takes operational command of the boat without being asked (Ch 14 cascade) reads richer with this layer: this is a man who has spent his life reading what is happening between the frames, and the cascade is a frame-break of the kind he has been training for in two distinct ways.

**On the boat:** he carries a small notebook for ostensibly scientific observations. In the back of the notebook — the back, where no one looks — there is a single panel of pencil work he started one quiet watch and has not finished. The drawing is of a hand. He cannot remember whose hand. He started it because the hand was holding a console-control in a particular way that struck him; he set the pencil down when his shift changed; the unfinished drawing is a record of one moment of attention that he allowed himself. He has not added to it. He does not show it to anyone. The drawing is private register; not duty register.

**Surfaces best in Ch 18** — the Punta Arenas reception is full of faces; Hiroshi is watching them all; Anna registers Hiroshi watching with an attention that is not entirely scientific. Earlier deployment is also possible (Ch 14 retroactive prose-pass: Hiroshi taking the helm with the manga-trained narrative-observation register layered under the chief-scientist register; Ch 17 transit-north prose-pass: Hiroshi in the wardroom margins quietly attentive). But Ch 18 is the first natural surfacing.

**Texture-detail discipline:** ONE mention per surfacing chapter. The notebook is a fact-in-scene; do NOT name "manga" in narration unless Anna has reason to know about Hiroshi's history (which she may; consortium gossip carries; senior scientists know each other's biographies); the unfinished panel is a fact about Hiroshi that Anna registers. Pairs structurally with Anna's bakery / Tashkent melon, Wanjiru's cucu-notebook, Diego's mate-cup, Joel's recipe-card — same "what-they-carry-against-cold" class.

**Influences (canon-light):** Tezuka Osamu (foundational); Otomo Katsuhiro (the *Akira* generation; influence later, when Hiroshi was already in university and the discipline was already lost); a third influence to be determined per chapter-drafter discretion if surfacing is ever extended.

**What he does NOT do:** does NOT lament; does NOT wish he had become a *mangaka*; does NOT name the suppression as failure. The discipline-of-suppression IS itself part of who he became; the choice was not regretted in the textbook sense; it was made; it is fact. The suppressed-art register is character-property, not character-grievance.

### The adventure-spirit register (CO directive 2026-05-06 refinement)

The manga-vocation and the polar-career share the same root impulse: a teenage boy who wanted to go where unknown things were and bring back proof. The genres a teenage Hiroshi was drawing in the late 1970s and early 1980s were ADVENTURE genres — Tezuka's *Phoenix* and the post-Tezuka shōnen tradition before he discovered Otomo Katsuhiro at university age and registered the post-apocalyptic adventure register *Akira* would become. **Adventure is the underlying register that connects manga-Hiroshi to glaciologist-Hiroshi.** What his parents redirected was the medium, not the impulse. Showa Station in the 1990s, the multiple JARE deployments, the Nansen mission at sixty: this is the adventure life he never stopped wanting. Glaciology gave him physical frontier the way manga would have given him narrative frontier; the impulse to go-where-others-have-not is the same.

This reframes several beats in his on-page register:

- **Ch 14 take-the-helm-without-being-asked beat:** the cascade is not a crisis Hiroshi is responding to; the cascade is the *frontier moment* his entire career has prepared him for. He moves toward the helm with the unflinching attention of a man who has spent his life moving toward the edge of physical knowledge. Not running toward it, not heroic posture — the way a manga hero in his draftsman's eye would have been drawn at the threshold of the next panel. This is *the moment that earns the page-turn*; he registers it as such; he does not name it as such; he takes the helm.
- **The polar deployments as career-long adventure execution:** thirty-plus years of going to the cold edge and bringing back glaciological data is not the consolation prize for the manga career he didn't have. It IS the adventure career he wanted, in scientifically institutionalized form. The Nansen mission at his age, in the Antarctic autumn, is a return to the register he has spent his life inside.
- **The unfinished-panel-in-the-back-of-the-notebook beat:** the panel he started during a quiet watch and never finished — it is a hand on a console-control. The hand belongs to someone in the middle of a frontier moment that has not yet broken. He started the drawing because he recognized the moment; he set it down because his shift changed and frontier moments do not wait. He carries the unfinished panel as a record of his observational discipline still functioning at the artistic register, even though the artistic register is decades dormant. The panel is incomplete because the moment was incomplete; it is faithful to what he saw.
- **His chief-scientist register at restraint:** Hiroshi is calm not because he lacks adventure-spirit but because he has been at the frontier long enough to know that adventure-spirit's correct register is restraint. The hero in the manga panel does not narrate his own heroism; the panel does the narration. Hiroshi has internalized this from both halves of his discipline.

**Surfacing implications:** when Hiroshi appears in scene during Vol 2's last chapters (Ch 17 transit-north; Ch 18 reception + cumulative reveal), the adventure-spirit-as-restraint register should be palpable in how he carries himself. He registers the post-mission contest the way a senior *mangaka* registers the next chapter's blank page — with anticipation, not anxiety. The standards-body institutional formation Wanjiru is seeding (Ch 17) is, from Hiroshi's angle, the next frontier — different medium, same impulse.

**Voice-pass note for any future chapter dispatch:** when writing Hiroshi, lean toward the under-stated adventurer register over the under-stated scientist register. Both are true; the former is more distinctive on the page. He has been to the cold edge; he intends to keep going; the manga he didn't draw and the glacier he did chart are the same person making the same choice across forty-five years.
