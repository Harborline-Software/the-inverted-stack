# Character Sheet — Senior Technical Specialist

## Identity

- **Name:** Placeholder. PAO recommendation: a Norwegian name with weight and texture. Candidates: Lars Bergmann, Henrik Solheim, Eivind Lund, Magnus Aas. Final to be locked during chapter draft.
- **Title in narrative:** Norwegian Polar Institute affiliation; civilian role on the mission.
- **Age at mission:** 58–63. The oldest crew member. The one everyone defers to on questions of polar conditions.
- **Nationality:** Norwegian.
- **Why Norwegian:** Three reasons. (1) Norway has the strongest civilian polar-research tradition in the West outside the US/UK; a Norwegian polar veteran is the most credible "this isn't supposed to happen to him" character. (2) Closes Barker's European market gap and adds geographic diversity to the crew without crowding the cast. (3) The death lands harder when it happens to the man the entire crew assumes is invulnerable to polar conditions — King's "make it specific to character" demand.

## Backstory (one paragraph)

Thirty-five years of polar oceanography. Started as a graduate student on Svalbard cruises in the late 1990s. Has been to both poles more than fifty times combined. Holds a Norwegian Polar Institute fellowship and a guest professorship at the University of Tromsø. Quiet professional reputation — he is famous inside the polar research community and unknown outside it. Authored or co-authored 140+ papers on Antarctic ice-shelf dynamics, including foundational work on the Filchner-Ronne system that this mission's instrumentation is designed to extend. Sunfish-1 was supposed to be his retirement mission. He told his wife it would be the last one before they bought the cabin in the Lofoten islands. He told the Director something different on departure night — that he wanted to see the Filchner-Ronne ice shelf from the underside one time before the climate makes it impossible.

He has photographed every Antarctic deployment of his career. Tens of thousands of frames. The mission's logbook entries are largely his — he keeps the kind of detailed observation record that other scientists pretend to keep. His handoff documentation is meticulous. His personal data on the boat's local-first system is deeply integrated with mission data because his mission work and his personal observation work are not separable.

## Personality / voice

- **Default mode:** Quiet, watchful, slow to speak, deliberate when he does. The kind of senior figure who shapes a watch by being on it, not by giving orders.
- **Voice register:** Spare, dry, occasionally ironic. Norwegian-accented English. Does not switch to Norwegian in mixed company.
- **Relationships on the boat:** Respected by all. The Director defers to him on polar questions. Maria likes him because he asks her about her work without being patronizing. The Lead Instrumentation Engineer trusts his calibration intuition more than her own.
- **Family:** Wife of 34 years, lives in Bergen. Two adult children in Oslo and Tromsø. A six-year-old granddaughter (PAO recommendation; lock during chapter draft) — old enough to remember him, young enough that his death will reshape her sense of who he was.

## What he wants

To finish the mission. To photograph the ice from the underside. To go home to his wife and the cabin in Lofoten.

## What he gets

He dies on bridge watch at 0317 local time, three hundred meters under the Filchner-Ronne ice shelf, halfway through the third week of the mission. Brain aneurysm. He does not see the ice from underneath in the way he wanted to. He had photographed it earlier in the dive, on the way down — those photographs are in the queue.

## His death — load-bearing structural notes

- **Cause:** Brain aneurysm. Sudden. No prior symptoms. Not preventable by any medical screening the mission did.
- **Setting:** Bridge watch, 0317. Quiet hour. He collapses. Watch officer calls medical. Maria arrives in 60 seconds. Resuscitation attempts continue 40 minutes. The body is on the bridge. The boat continues to operate.
- **What happens to the body:** Goes to the freezer behind the dogged door. Standard naval-vessel procedure for on-duty deaths during transit. The freezer is a real thing on real submarines; the procedure is real. The reader does not need to be told it is real for it to feel real.
- **What happens to his work:** Mission logs, photographs, observation records, handoff documentation, an unfinished paper on Filchner-Ronne ice-shelf basal melt rates. All on the boat's local-first system. All signed with his Ed25519 device key. All queued for the next surface relay window.
- **What the architecture preserves:** Everything. His last photograph from the dive. His last log entry, written 40 minutes before he collapsed. The handoff documentation he prepared in case anyone needed to take over his observation runs. The architecture does not bring him back. The architecture delivers what he did.
- **What reaches his family:** When the queue resumes (Act IV → Act V), the death notification, the personal letter the Director composed, his photographs, his logs, his unfinished paper, his handoff documentation — all reach his wife at 1442 Norway local time, eleven days after his death. The architecture is the difference between his family receiving everything and his family receiving a locked phone.

## Role in each act

- **Act I (Departure):** Introduced as the senior figure. Brief sketch of his polar career. The Director's voice on him: "I have been deferring to him on polar conditions for ten years. I will defer to him on this mission too."
- **Act II (Submerged Transit):** Present in daily watch rotation. One scene of him photographing instrument calibration through the boat's viewport. One conversation with the Director where he tells her something important about her own command — placeholder content, lock during chapter draft.
- **Act III (The Death — present tense set-piece):** Dies. Forty minutes of resuscitation. Body to the freezer.
- **Act IV (Silent Window):** Absent (deceased). His name appears in the Director's interior monologue — she walks past the freezer; she imagines telling his wife; the relay window closes silent and his work does not move.
- **Act V (Return):** His work reaches his family. The Director's staff history closing pages dedicate one paragraph to him: what he photographed, what he observed, what he was about to publish, what his daughter and his granddaughter received.

## What this character does NOT do

- Does NOT have a death-bed speech. The aneurysm is sudden. He does not get last words.
- Does NOT have a redemption arc. He does not need one. He is doing his work when he dies. The chapter honors that fact.
- Does NOT die heroically. The aneurysm is medical, not dramatic. The architecture's value is shown by what survives him, not by how he died.
- Does NOT echo Charlie. The reader who read Ch22 will hear the resonance — a man dies, his work is endangered, his family waits — but the chapter does not state it. The Senior Technical Specialist is his own character with his own family and his own life. He is not a stand-in.
