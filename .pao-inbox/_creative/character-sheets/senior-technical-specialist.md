# Character Sheet — Senior Technical Specialist

## Identity

- **Name:** **LOCKED:** Diego Vargas. (Previously Lars Bergmann placeholder — Norwegian. CO-directive swap to Argentine to close LATAM-beyond-Brazil gap and anchor the death-scene in a research tradition that predates every cloud company in the book.)
- **Title in narrative:** **Instituto Antártico Argentino (IAA)** affiliation; civilian role on the mission. Guest professorship at the **University of Buenos Aires** (UBA), Faculty of Exact and Natural Sciences.
- **Age at mission:** 58–63. The oldest crew member. The one everyone defers to on questions of polar conditions.
- **Nationality:** Argentine (born in Buenos Aires; some childhood years in Ushuaia / Tierra del Fuego per father's posting; family home in Buenos Aires through adulthood).
- **Why Argentine:** Three reasons. (1) **Argentina has the longest continuous Antarctic presence on Earth** — the IAA was founded in 1951, but Argentine year-round Antarctic occupation goes back to **Orcadas Station, 1904**, an unbroken 124-year continuity by the time of the mission. A senior Argentine polar scientist is the most credible "this isn't supposed to happen to him" character because his country has been doing this longer than any other. (2) Closes the LATAM-beyond-Brazil gap (we already have Brazilian co-funding referenced in Sabina's portfolio; an Argentine *crew member* gives Latin America an embodied seat at the table). (3) The death lands harder when it happens to the man whose institution has run continuous Antarctic operations since the year of the Wright Brothers' first flight — King's "make it specific to character" demand. The IAA's institutional memory is older than aviation.

## Backstory (one paragraph)

Thirty-five years of polar oceanography. Started as a graduate student on Argentine Antarctic resupply rotations in the late 1990s, working out of **Estación Marambio** and later **Base Esperanza** — the IAA station where Argentine families have wintered continuously since 1978 and where the first child born on the Antarctic continent (Emilio Marcos Palma, 1978) has the same hometown registry as the Argentine national archive. Has been to both poles more than fifty times combined. Holds an IAA senior research fellowship and a guest professorship at the **University of Buenos Aires**, Faculty of Exact and Natural Sciences. Quiet professional reputation — he is famous inside the polar research community and unknown outside it. Authored or co-authored 140+ papers on Antarctic ice-shelf dynamics, including foundational work on the Filchner-Ronne system that this mission's instrumentation is designed to extend; co-authored several with Brazilian PROANTAR colleagues and with the Chilean INACH program (the South American Antarctic research consortium is small and tightly networked). Sunfish-1 was supposed to be his retirement mission. He told his wife it would be the last one before they bought the small house in **San Martín de los Andes** (Patagonian lake district — the Argentine equivalent of a Lofoten cabin in scale and emotional weight: a place to fish, to read, to disappear with grandchildren). He told the Director something different on departure night — that he wanted to see the Filchner-Ronne ice shelf from the underside one time before the climate makes it impossible.

He has photographed every Antarctic deployment of his career. Tens of thousands of frames. The mission's logbook entries are largely his — he keeps the kind of detailed observation record that other scientists pretend to keep. His handoff documentation is meticulous. His personal data on the boat's local-first system is deeply integrated with mission data because his mission work and his personal observation work are not separable.

## Personality / voice

- **Default mode:** Quiet, watchful, slow to speak, deliberate when he does. The kind of senior figure who shapes a watch by being on it, not by giving orders.
- **Voice register:** Spare, dry, occasionally ironic. Argentine-Spanish-accented English (porteño cadence audible on technical English; Spanish in private conversation with himself or with Wanjiru's Latin-American Spanish from East African development circuits). Does not switch to Spanish in mixed company unless the moment calls for it.
- **Relationships on the boat:** Respected by all. The Director defers to him on polar questions. Maria likes him because he asks her about her work without being patronizing. The Lead Instrumentation Engineer (Priya) trusts his calibration intuition more than her own.
- **Family:** Wife of 34 years (**María Elena Vargas**, née Castelli — Italian-Argentine; the country is full of them; **LOCKED**), lives in **Buenos Aires** (Belgrano neighborhood). Two adult children in Buenos Aires and Bariloche (the second daughter is a CONICET-affiliated biologist in Patagonia — research runs in the family). A six-year-old granddaughter (**Sofía Vargas**, **LOCKED**) — old enough to remember him, young enough that his death will reshape her sense of who he was.

## What he wants

To finish the mission. To photograph the ice from the underside. To go home to his wife and the small house they were going to buy in San Martín de los Andes.

## What he gets

He dies on bridge watch at 0317 local time, three hundred meters under the Filchner-Ronne ice shelf, halfway through the third week of the mission. Brain aneurysm. He does not see the ice from underneath in the way he wanted to. He had photographed it earlier in the dive, on the way down — those photographs are in the queue.

## His death — load-bearing structural notes

- **Cause:** Brain aneurysm. Sudden. No prior symptoms. Not preventable by any medical screening the mission did.
- **Setting:** Bridge watch, 0317. Quiet hour. He collapses. Watch officer (Joel Reyes — Filipino life-support engineer, see `_minor-characters.md`) calls medical. Maria arrives in 60 seconds. Resuscitation attempts continue 40 minutes. The body is on the bridge. The boat continues to operate.
- **What happens to the body:** Goes to the freezer behind the dogged door. Standard naval-vessel procedure for on-duty deaths during transit. The freezer is a real thing on real submarines; the procedure is real. The reader does not need to be told it is real for it to feel real.
- **What happens to his work:** Mission logs, photographs, observation records, handoff documentation, an unfinished paper on Filchner-Ronne ice-shelf basal melt rates (co-authored with two PROANTAR colleagues at the Universidade Federal do Rio Grande do Sul). All on the boat's local-first system. All signed with his Ed25519 device key. All queued for the next surface relay window.
- **What the architecture preserves:** Everything. His last photograph from the dive. His last log entry, written 40 minutes before he collapsed. The handoff documentation he prepared in case anyone needed to take over his observation runs. The architecture does not bring him back. The architecture delivers what he did.
- **What reaches his family:** When the queue resumes (Act IV → Act V), the death notification, the personal letter the Director composed (in Spanish, because Anna's Russian-language teaching career means she has more languages than most flag officers and because the letter is for María Elena, not for the IAA's communications office), his photographs, his logs, his unfinished paper, his handoff documentation — all reach his wife at **1442 Argentina local time**, eleven days after his death. The architecture is the difference between his family receiving everything and his family receiving a locked phone.

## Role in each act

- **Act I (Departure):** Introduced as the senior figure. Brief sketch of his polar career. The Director's voice on him: *"I have been deferring to him on polar conditions for ten years. I will defer to him on this mission too. The Argentine Antarctic Institute has had a station occupied without interruption since 1904. He carries that institutional memory in his bones. He does not announce it. He does not need to."*
- **Act II (Submerged Transit):** Present in daily watch rotation. One scene of him photographing instrument calibration through the boat's viewport (Priya's instruments, his lens). One conversation with the Director where he tells her something important about her own command — placeholder content, lock during chapter draft.
- **Act III (The Death — present tense set-piece):** Dies. Forty minutes of resuscitation. Body to the freezer. Joel Reyes is the watch officer who calls medical; Maria runs the resuscitation; Hiroshi Nakamura takes operational command of the boat while the Director writes the family letter.
- **Act IV (Silent Window):** Absent (deceased). His name appears in the Director's interior monologue — she walks past the freezer; she imagines telling María Elena; the relay window closes silent and his work does not move.
- **Act V (Return):** His work reaches his family. The Director's staff history closing pages dedicate one paragraph to him: what he photographed, what he observed, what he was about to publish, what María Elena and Sofía received.

## What this character does NOT do

- Does NOT have a death-bed speech. The aneurysm is sudden. He does not get last words.
- Does NOT have a redemption arc. He does not need one. He is doing his work when he dies. The chapter honors that fact.
- Does NOT die heroically. The aneurysm is medical, not dramatic. The architecture's value is shown by what survives him, not by how he died.
- Does NOT echo Charlie. The reader who read Ch22 will hear the resonance — a man dies, his work is endangered, his family waits — but the chapter does not state it. Diego Vargas is his own character with his own family and his own life. He is not a stand-in.
- Does NOT generalize "Latin America." He is Argentine specifically. The IAA is specifically. Buenos Aires is specifically. San Martín de los Andes is specifically. The granddaughter has a name. King-rule: every detail particular.
