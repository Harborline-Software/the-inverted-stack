## Physical texture during operations (CO directive 2026-05-05 craft note)

**Visibly relaxed rigor.** Wanjiru's body shows equanimity even when stakes are operationally critical. Concrete patterns:

- She **rarely raises her voice even when drawing hard lines** — her *no* is calm, her *yes-with-conditions* is calm, her *I will not authorize that* is calm; the discipline is in the calm, not in the volume

- When she is working a forensic query or drafting a regulatory filing, her body is **still** — focused, breathing slow, gestures economical
- She makes eye contact when she is delivering an institutional decision; she looks away when she is thinking, not when she is concealing

The reader can identify Wanjiru in a paragraph without dialogue tags by the *equanimity* in her presence — and by the hum.

## Fear-axis (CO directive 2026-05-05 craft note)

**Wanjiru's fear is being wrong politically / user-impact.** Concrete shape:
- The framework she's drafting failing the people it was supposed to protect
- A revocation policy that is technically correct but operationally unjust — denying access to someone who should have it, granting access to someone who shouldn't, in a way that the institution then locks in as precedent

## Backstory — biographical firewall observed

**Pre-Sunfish trajectory (PAO writes; informed-by patterns of real Kenyan fintech-engineering professional life):**

University of Nairobi or Strathmore University — computer science or software engineering. Came up through the **M-PESA-adjacent ecosystem** in her early career: Safaricom's mobile-money infrastructure team or one of the consultancies that built around it (Cellulant, Craft Silicon, the various integrators) or one of the next-generation Kenyan fintech companies (Branch, Tala, M-KOPA's lending arm). The specific employer matters less than the operational reality: she came up in a system that had to handle financial transactions across rural Kenya, where connectivity was unreliable and the consequences of getting it wrong were measured in livelihoods.

The lessons she absorbed from M-PESA's institutional discipline — and from the broader Kenyan mobile-money sector that grew up around it after the 2007 launch — are what make her a Saidar channeler. M-PESA was offline-first finance at population scale before the academic local-first community gave the pattern a name. Operators in that ecosystem learned: the data is livelihood; access decisions have stakes; key-rotation has to work for users who don't have the cognitive bandwidth to manage it themselves; the system has to forgive. The political dimension of security — *whose data, under what authority, with what recourse* — was operational reality from day one.

Wanjiru carried that thinking into Sunfish OSS contribution starting around 2022. She filed bug reports against the early security architecture. The reports specified attack scenarios drawn from real Kenyan fintech threat models — not theoretical academic adversaries, but the actual patterns that mobile-money operators see (SIM-swap attacks, agent-side fraud, jurisdictional pressure on user data when subpoena meets sovereignty, key compromise under physical-coercion threat models that don't show up in U.S./European literature).

Her contributions evolved from bug reports → patches → design proposals → co-authorship on the security-architecture chapter. By 2024 she was the **principal author of the key-management mechanism** — the architecture that became Vol 1 Ch15 §Key-Loss Recovery + the broader key hierarchy + role architecture.

The R1 council found a BLOCK on key compromise. Specifically: under the original mechanism, a revoked device whose ciphertext was already in transit could not be reliably prevented from completing decryption upon receipt — the revocation broadcast and the in-flight ciphertext race condition was undefined, and "undefined" is not safe in a security model that has to defend against deliberately crafted timing attacks.

Wanjiru's R2 rewrite resolved this. The mechanism became more complex; the rewrite required the receiver-side decryption to validate against a current revocation horizon held in the local store before completing the decrypt operation, and the revocation horizon to be propagated through the gossip layer with explicit acknowledgment from a quorum of receivers before a revocation could be considered effective. The clearance with conditions is the canonical key-compromise spec.

**the Nansen selection:**

Anna picked Wanjiru specifically. The relay operations on the Nansen use *Wanjiru's* key-management architecture; the access-control decisions during the mission run on *Wanjiru's* role architecture; the security model that holds during the leak event is the model *Wanjiru* designed for political fragility under coercion-threat conditions. The fact that her assumptions for political fragility happen to be exactly right for fire-and-flood on a submarine is one of the architectural surprises of the Nansen — security models written to defend against governance threats turn out to defend equally well against environmental ones. The structural insight: *the kind of failures that destroy data are similar at the architectural layer regardless of whether the cause is political or physical.*

## The R1 BLOCK on key compromise (key flashback chapter)

The council's R1 review surfaced a BLOCK on Wanjiru's key-management mechanism: the revocation-broadcast-vs-in-flight-ciphertext race condition. A revoked device whose ciphertext was already in transit could not be reliably prevented from completing decryption upon receipt; the original mechanism had no defined behavior for this case.

Wanjiru's response was a sustained design pass — three weeks, like Priya's. Unlike Priya, she did not need to build a reproduction case; she had seen this exact failure mode in M-PESA's threat-model literature, in the SIM-swap attack pattern. What she had failed to do was *transfer the recognition* from her fintech operational knowledge into her Sunfish architectural design. The R1 BLOCK was, for her, a moment of recognition: she had not channeled all of what she already knew into the mechanism.

The R2 rewrite became the receiver-side validation against a local-store revocation horizon, with explicit gossip-layer propagation requirements and quorum-acknowledgment before revocation effectiveness. The mechanism became more complex; the additional propagation latency was the price; the architecture cleared with conditions.

Notes for the eventual draft:
- **Setting:** Wanjiru's home office in Nairobi (or wherever her domicile lands). The R1 BLOCK printout on her desk; she reads it and recognizes the failure mode within twenty minutes — the M-PESA pattern is muscle memory; she had simply not transferred it.
- **The interior:** Wanjiru's specific work is *transfer*. She has the operational knowledge; the architecture needs it expressed as a mechanism. The three weeks are the time it takes to find the right shape — not to invent the solution, but to recognize the solution she already carried.
- **The fix delivery:** like Joel, she leads with the rewrite. Unlike Joel, she names the M-PESA lineage in the cover note. *I had not transferred the SIM-swap pattern from my fintech work into the architecture. The original mechanism assumed the revocation broadcast and the in-flight ciphertext were sequenced; in production they race. The rewrite makes the receiver validate against a local revocation horizon with quorum-acknowledged propagation. Please review.*
- **The acceptance:** the council clears with conditions. The mechanism becomes the canonical key-compromise spec.
- **Length target:** 1500-2000 words.
