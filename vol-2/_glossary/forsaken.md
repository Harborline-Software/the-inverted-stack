# Forsaken (Vol 2 narrative-arc proper noun)

**Plain replacement:** **KEEP** — proper noun. Do not substitute. The entry exists for reader/listener orientation, not replacement.

**Longer gloss:** *In Vol 2, "Forsaken" is the in-universe name for the rival narrative being mounted on the surface while the Nansen is submerged — a regulatory and PR campaign that frames local-first architecture as something to be left behind. Each "Forsaken Reveal" chapter is a moment when Anna and Wanjiru, surfacing for a brief comms window, learn what the surface has been saying about their work in their absence.* (use at first chapter-title encounter — Ch 6 *First Surface, First Forsaken Reveal* — or in the chapter epigraph / front matter if one is added)

**Audio replacement:**
- *Forsaken* (chapter title or proper-noun usage) → *the Forsaken narrative* or *the Forsaken position* on first encounter in audio. The narrator should also pause briefly before the word so the listener registers it as a name, not the adjective.
- *First Forsaken Reveal* (Ch 6 title) → *First Forsaken-narrative Reveal* on the title read. Pacing-driven; the hyphenation is a cue to the audio narrator, not a print convention.
- *Second Forsaken Reveal* (Ch 11 title) → same treatment.
- *the cumulative Forsaken reveal* (CHAPTER-OUTLINE language; if it surfaces in prose) → *the cumulative reveal of the Forsaken narrative*

**Keep when:**
- ALL occurrences in chapter titles, in-universe text, character dialogue, and Anna's filed log entries. The word is canonical; replacement would erase a Vol 2 narrative anchor.
- Cross-references to the `.pao-inbox/_creative/forsaken-position-papers/` source documents (the in-universe artifacts that define the Forsaken position).

**Mistaken for:**
- The adjective (*she felt forsaken*, *a forsaken outpost*) — the dominant audio collision. Without the modifier *narrative* or *position*, the listener will hear the title *First Forsaken Reveal* as *first abandoned reveal* or *first lonely reveal* and parse the chapter as having a melancholic atmosphere rather than a narrative-political event.
- *God-forsaken* / religious-forsaken senses — distinct register entirely; the rival narrative is institutional and regulatory, not theological.
- A specific product or platform name (Forsaken is NOT a product like Sunfish or a competing platform like a hypothetical *ForsakenOS*) — it's a discourse / framing / political position. The longer gloss is essential precisely because there is no concrete object the word names.

**Feynman test:** *Could a 10-year-old picture this?* Yes — "imagine a rival sports team has chosen a name that is also a kind of accusation: they call themselves something dramatic to make a point about how they see the world. *The Forsaken* is the name a rival camp uses to make a political claim — that the people they represent have been left behind by the way other people build software, and that their alternative is the answer." The rival-team-with-a-pointed-name image is the workshop entry's load-bearing element.
