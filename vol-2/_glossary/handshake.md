# Handshake

**Plain replacement:** *the verification exchange* · *the identity check* · *the verification handshake* (when retaining the technical noun adds clarity)

**Longer gloss:** *Before two computer systems agree to talk to each other, they exchange a few short messages to prove they each are who they claim to be and to settle on the rules for the conversation that follows. That short exchange is called a handshake.* (use at first occurrence in any chapter where the term is doing structural work - Ch 6 first surface, Ch 9 sync-daemon triage, Ch 13 schema-version handshake)

**Audio replacement:**
- *handshake* (technical sense) → *the verification exchange* (audio always expands; the listener hears *handshake* and pictures two people shaking hands)
- *schema-version handshake* → already covered by `schema-migration.md` substitution (*record-version check*)
- *the gossip protocol's reconciliation cycle* / *handshake completed* / *handshake failed* → use *verification exchange completed* / *verification exchange failed*
- *handshake* in any non-technical sense (a literal handshake between people) → leave alone

**Keep when:**
- Engineer dialogue (Wanjiru, Joel, Priya) when one engineer briefs another - engineer voice marker. Audio still expands at first occurrence.
- Anna's filed log entries where the formal procedural register requires the technical noun.
- Cross-references to Vol 1 Ch 14 *Sync Daemon Protocol* where *handshake* is the canonical term.

**Mistaken for:**
- *Handshake* (the social greeting) - direct collision; non-technical reader/listener will picture two people shaking hands rather than two systems exchanging verification messages. The longer gloss is essential at first occurrence.
- *Agreement* (a finalized deal) - a handshake is the *opening* exchange that establishes the agreement; the work that follows is what the agreement enables. Don't treat *handshake* as *agreement*.

**Feynman test:** *Could a 10-year-old picture this?* Yes - "imagine two kids who want to swap snacks meet at the playground and say a secret password to each other: each says their half; only if both halves match does the swap happen. The handshake is the password exchange before the actual swap." The two-kids-with-a-secret-password image is the workshop entry's load-bearing element.
