# Timestamp

**Plain replacement:** *the date-and-time stamp* · *the timestamp* (the term is plain enough; light entry)

**Longer gloss:** *A timestamp is a precise moment in time recorded as part of a piece of data — usually accurate to the second, sometimes to the millisecond. The architecture stamps every action and every record with the moment it happened, so later the system can reconstruct what happened in what order.* (use at first occurrence in any chapter where the term is doing structural work — Vol 2 surfaces *timestamp* in audit-log scenes mostly)

**Audio replacement:**
- *timestamp* → leave alone (the term is plain enough on audio; the listener's anchor — *the timestamp on the photo*, *the timestamp on the email* — is correct).
- ISO 8601 timestamps (e.g., *2026-09-15T11:18*) → handled by the predecessor subagent's `narratable_text()` fix; expanded to spoken form (*September 15, 2026, at 11:18*).

**Keep when:**
- ALL occurrences. The print word is plain enough; the audio expansion happens at the lower-level ISO-8601 transformation, not via this workshop entry.

**Mistaken for:**
- Nothing significant. *Timestamp* is one of the few technical-vocabulary words where the everyday anchor is correct.

**Feynman test:** *Could a 10-year-old picture this?* Yes — "imagine every photo on a phone has the date and time it was taken stamped invisibly into the picture, so years later the phone can sort all the photos by when they happened. A timestamp is that invisible stamp, applied to data instead of photos." The photo-with-invisible-date image is the workshop entry's load-bearing element.
