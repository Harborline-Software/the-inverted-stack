# Manifest (manifest signature)

**Plain replacement:** *the inventory document* · *the listing* · *the signed inventory* (when the cryptographic signature property matters) · *the manifest* (the term is plain enough on second occurrence)

**Longer gloss:** *A manifest is a written list of what something contains, alongside a stamp that proves the list itself hasn't been altered since it was signed. When a piece of software arrives at the boat - a probe firmware update, a configuration change, a model file - it travels with a manifest that catalogs everything inside it and a signature that proves the catalog is genuine.* (use at first occurrence in any chapter where the term is doing structural work - Ch 13 schema-migration crisis [Joel verified the manifest signature], Ch 15 compromised-relay aftermath)

**Audio replacement:**
- *manifest* (technical sense) → *the inventory document* on first occurrence in scene; *the manifest* afterwards
- *manifest signature* → *the cryptographic stamp on the inventory*
- *signed manifest* → *the inventory with its cryptographic stamp*
- *crew manifest* (the everyday sense; e.g., *every officer on the manifest*) → leave alone; the meaning is clear from context

**Keep when:**
- Engineer dialogue when *manifest* is the canonical noun.
- Anna's filed log entries.
- The phrase *the calmest man on the manifest* (Ch 1 Hiroshi) - uses the everyday sense (*crew manifest*); this is preserved untouched.

**Mistaken for:**
- *Crew manifest* / *cargo manifest* - the closest everyday meaning; partial transfer is helpful but the cryptographic signature property is what makes the technical sense distinct.
- *Manifesto* - distinct word; the listener may mishear *manifest* as *manifesto*. Audio narrator should pace the word clearly.
- *Manifest destiny* - irrelevant association; flag it as a mishearing risk if the listener seems to drift.

**Feynman test:** *Could a 10-year-old picture this?* Yes - "imagine a moving truck arrives at your house with a list taped to the side: every box, every piece of furniture, every item inside, written down. At the bottom of the list is the moving company's stamp. If the list says 'twelve boxes' and you only see eleven, the stamp tells you who to ask. The manifest is that list, and the signature is the stamp." The moving-truck-with-a-list-on-the-side image is the workshop entry's load-bearing element.
