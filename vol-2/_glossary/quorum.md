# Quorum (two-party / three-party / read-confirmation)

**Plain replacement:** *the agreement of three* (when three-party quorum) · *the three-party check* · *the agreement of two* (when two-party) · *the joint confirmation* (generic)

**Longer gloss:** *In the architecture, certain operations only count as valid if a minimum number of independent parties - usually three - have each confirmed they see the same thing. That minimum is called a quorum. The architecture refuses to roll forward until the quorum agrees.* (use at first occurrence in any chapter where the concept is doing structural work - Ch 13 schema-migration four-pass cycle, Ch 14 leak event)

**Audio replacement:**
- *quorum* → *the agreement of three* or *the three-party check* (audio always expands; the parliamentary association is partial transfer at best - listener may know quorum from school-board meetings, but the technical sense is more specific)
- *three-party read-confirmation* → *three-party check that all three see the same thing*
- *two-party* / *three-party* (used adjectivally) → leave alone if context already established; otherwise expand to *two-witness* / *three-witness*
- *read-confirmation* → *the read check* or *the confirmation check*

**Keep when:**
- Engineer dialogue when *quorum* is the precise technical noun (Priya's Ch 13 refusal-of-record uses *three-party read-confirmation* in formal procedural register).
- Anna's filed log entries - the formal voice carries the technical term.
- Cross-references to Vol 1 Ch 14 *Sync Daemon Protocol* and Ch 6 *The Distributed Systems Lens* where *quorum* is the canonical name.

**Mistaken for:**
- *Quorum* (parliamentary - minimum members needed to vote) - partial transfer; the technical sense is similar but more specific (it's about read-confirmation across systems, not about voting).
- *Majority* - distinct concept; quorum is a minimum threshold, not a majority. Three parties out of five is a quorum; five out of nine is a majority. Don't conflate.
- *Consensus* - distinct concept; consensus means everyone agrees; a quorum is the minimum number whose agreement is required. Don't substitute *consensus* for *quorum*.

**Feynman test:** *Could a 10-year-old picture this?* Yes - "imagine a treasure chest that needs three different keys turned at the same time to open. Even if you have two of the keys, the chest stays locked. The three keys turning together is the quorum. The chest only opens when the agreement happens." The three-keys-turned-at-once image is the workshop entry's load-bearing element.
