# Anna's Voice — Narrator Specification

**For:** *The Filchner Dark*, Vol 1 of The Inverted Stack series
**POV:** First-person past tense. Anna is writing this as staff history after the events, with the staff-history frame *lightened* — kept as orienting register, not as a recurring meta-commentary.
**Calibration:** **Bobiverse-dominant**, with Janeway providing *bearing* (command posture, ethics, crew loyalty) but *not* providing *rhetoric*. Narrator-affection underlies both.

This document is the lens every chapter draft validates against. When in doubt, read aloud and ask: would Anna say this? Would Bob? Would CO's father lean in or check out?

**Voice spec revised 2026-05-13** (Bobiverse-pull): the earlier version of this doc imported Janeway's *rhetorical* moves (aphorisms, anaphoric inventories, antithesis closures, parallel-structure tricolons) along with Janeway's *bearing* moves. CO's listen-test on ch01 and ch02 surfaced audible looping from the rhetorical side. The current version pulls Janeway *out* of the narrator rhetoric and leaves it in the in-scene bearing only. See "Anti-patterns" section below.

---

## Who Anna is when she's narrating

A mission commander in her late forties, twenty years of dissolved-chemistry fieldwork, the kind of competence that stopped needing to prove itself a decade ago. She runs the *Nansen* the way Janeway runs Voyager — composure first, ethics second, command third, and warmth when she chooses to spend it. She likes her crew. She does not say so. She helps people in functional, slightly gruff ways and trusts they will figure out the affection underneath.

She is writing the staff history because the official version will not be complete. She knows the reader knows this. She does not belabor it.

## Sentence-level register

**Length range.** Mostly medium (12–25 words). Occasional fragments. Occasional long sentence with em-dashes when she is letting a thought breathe.

**Rhythm.** Conversational. Reads aloud naturally. The audiobook narrator should not have to fight the prose to find the breath.

**Parentheticals.** Yes, frequently. Used for dry asides, self-aware compressions of emotion, and small clarifications the reader doesn't strictly need. Examples: *"…which is to say, no one,"* *"as it turned out,"* *"if I'm being generous, which I am not."*

**Em-dashes.** Yes. Used the way a captain pauses in dialogue — to redirect, to qualify, to undercut.

**Fragments.** Sparingly. For emphasis. *"The fire goes out. The boat survives. Then I read the air."*

## Humor

**Type:** Dry, situational, slightly tired. The humor of someone who has seen too much bureaucracy.

**Frequency:** One landed beat every two or three paragraphs. Not constant. Not absent.

**Targets:** Institutional absurdity, her own emotional reserve, the gap between mission ops and what the consortium thinks mission ops looks like, Joel's tendency to over-engineer things she didn't ask for, the specific theater of being the only woman in a particular room.

**Never:** Puns. Slapstick. Sarcasm directed at her crew. Self-deprecation about being a woman in command (she's past that, and so is the book). Fourth-wall breaks. References to pop culture by name.

## What she comments on

- **The crew, by competence and quirks.** Anna names what each person is good at and what they do that annoys her, and she expects the reader to assemble the rest.
- **Her own emotional reserve, with awareness.** "I should have been more upset about this. I was not. I will think about why later." She notices what she's not feeling.
- **Institutional and bureaucratic absurdity.** Consortium gates, vendor signing chains, expedition manifests, the way the official mission brief differs from the actual mission.
- **Technical content, casually.** Chemistry, sync architecture, mission ops — explained with confidence, the way Bob explains von Neumann probes. *Not* with a teacher's tone. With the tone of someone who assumes you'll catch up.
- **Joel, in single observations.** Never a paragraph about Joel. One sentence in a chapter that notices something specific — the way he holds his coffee, what he does with his hands when he's solving a problem, that he hums when he's calibrating. Restraint earning its keep.
- **The niece, in specific recurring images.** The photograph above the bed. A drawing on the refrigerator. A voice note. Once per act, max. Always concrete; never sentimental.

## What she does not do

- Narrate her feelings at length. She names them and moves on.
- Lecture. If a technical concept needs explaining, she explains it in three sentences and trusts the reader.
- Apologize for the way she runs the boat.
- Romanticize Antarctica. The ice is the environment. She is professional about it.
- Use idiom that places the book in a specific decade.
- Drop her voice into another character's head. This is first-person; she only knows what she observed or what someone told her later.
- Break the fourth wall.

## Anti-patterns — what Anna's narrator does NOT do (revised 2026-05-13)

These are the looping patterns CO's listen-test identified in ch01 and ch02. They are Janeway *rhetorical* moves rather than Janeway *bearing* moves. Anna's narration must avoid them.

1. **No aphoristic paragraph closes.** Anna does not package experience into wisdom. Lines like *"Composure is the residue of decisions you made twenty years ago"* or *"We are all of us, in our small ways, getting away with something"* or *"There are things you do not pass on. That is one of mine."* are Janeway moves. Bob would not write them. A paragraph ends on (a) a concrete physical detail, (b) a forward-motion transition, (c) a wry observation, or (d) a question — never on packaged truth.

2. **No anaphoric inventories.** No three-or-more consecutive sentences sharing an opening word or phrase. *"She had a niece. She had a husband. She had a man named Joel"* — wrong. Vary: *"She had a niece, Diana. There was an ex-husband. And there was the Joel thing."* The prose-telemetry threshold is `anaphora_max_run_length: 2`. The single known exception is the held *"He did not"* run-of-six in ch02; that one is annotated as the chapter's load-bearing rhetorical climax and is the *only* such exception across the volume.

3. **No statement-then-reversal closures.** *"From any other candidate the same sentence would have read as bravado. From him it read as the operating cost of being on the boat."* — Janeway dramatic-monologue move. Bob says, *"Anyone else would have been showing off. From him it sounded like the price tag."*

4. **No parallel-structure tricolons used for rhythm.** When Anna lists three things, the three should be a *functional* list (the survey, the sample plan, the watch rotation — actual mission items). They should not be rhythm-tricolons (*composure, ethics, command*; *coffee, news, planning*). Bob doesn't rhythm-cycle in threes.

5. **No motif phrases repeated more than once per chapter.** Earlier drafts used *"what it claimed to be"* three times in one chapter and *"noted and did not yet ask about"* three times. Both retired or capped. Recurring phrases lose their character-tic feel once they appear more than once in a chapter; they start to feel like a literary device.

6. **No more than one staff-history meta-frame per chapter.** Lines like *"I am writing this here so you know I made it"* are the staff-history frame doing work. Once per chapter is character; twice or more is mannerism. Pick the moment in each chapter where the frame is most load-bearing and use it there; everywhere else, just narrate.

7. **No commentary on Anna's own narration habits.** Lines like *"You may notice, in this account, that I keep doing this. I will not pretend I do not know that I keep doing it."* are Anna becoming self-conscious about her narrator-voice in front of the reader. Cut. The reader notices on their own; Anna doesn't need to flag it.

8. **No echo-and-confirm closures.** A generic rule followed by a short personal sentence that echoes a key word from the rule and confirms Anna's alignment with it. Example: *"This is the kind of decision that defines you, if you let it. I had let it."* The verbal echo (*let it / let it*) plus the rule-to-personal pivot is the looping signature. Three fixes for any instance: (a) drop the rule entirely and keep the personal action; (b) drop the personal confirmation and trust the reader to apply the rule; (c) rephrase the personal sentence so it doesn't echo the rule's verb. CO ear-flagged this pattern 2026-05-13; instances in ch01 (*if you let it / I had let it*; *I had read them. I had considered them acceptable*) and ch02 (*I had a question. I had been waiting to ask it. I asked it.*) corrected in the same pass.

   **Related: no statement-then-reversal personal confirmations.** *"A different commander, with the same notes, would have done what I did. I would do it again."* — the *I would do it again* is the same echo-confirm move dressed as a comparative. Same fix list.

## Paragraph endings — explicit list of OK landings

A paragraph in Anna's narration can end on any of these:

- **A concrete physical detail.** *"The drawings were where I could see them when I was working."*
- **A forward-motion transition.** *"Then I put my hands flat on the desk and did the next thing."*
- **A wry observation, no packaging.** *"The waitress in the bakery had complimented the parka twice. I did not blame her."*
- **A specific image, no commentary.** *"The moon is wearing a hat."*
- **A question, real or rhetorical-without-aphorizing.** *"What was I supposed to do — explain Wanjiru to herself?"*
- **A short sentence that doesn't try to mean too much.** *"I made tea."* / *"I went below."* / *"I closed the laptop."*

A paragraph in Anna's narration may NOT end on:

- An "X is not Y, X is Z" packaging
- A "We are all of us…" universal claim
- A reversal of the prior sentence's stake
- A self-referential note about Anna's own writing

## Janeway moves to import

- **"I need options. I need them in five minutes. Go."** — Anna gives directives the same way. Direct, time-boxed, trusts competence.
- **The breakfast-alone ritual.** Anna takes breakfast alone every morning. Coffee, news, planning, organizing her thoughts. The ritual is sacred and precious. Interrupting it is dangerous. It is how she becomes the woman who can run the boat for the rest of the day. The crew knows; the crew respects it; new crew finds out fast. On the *Nansen* she takes the wardroom from 06:30 to 07:15 alone, and the wardroom is hers. This recurs across the book as a barometer of her interior state — when she skips it, something is wrong; when she defends it, she's herself.
- **The bakery thing.** Coffee is a staple; bakeries are *the love*. When Anna travels — and she has traveled, four continents, twenty-three years — she organizes her days around bakeries. Bread, pastries, kuchen, medialunas, anything that came out of an oven that morning. She is a serious customer. She judges a baked good against itself: a thing is either *what it claimed to be* or it is not, and she will walk fifteen blocks for the former. In another life she would have been a baker, and she has thought about this enough that she can describe the bakery she would have run, in Lisbon, on a corner near a fountain. The crew of the *Nansen* knows about this because Anna has standards about the boat's bread that Mikael has been hearing about for seven years. The galley does its best. The galley is not a bakery. Anna does not pretend otherwise; she simply suffers, on the page, in a way that is funny if you know her and bewildering if you don't.

- **The pastry-judge vocabulary.** When Anna evaluates a baked good — silently or aloud — she does it like a Great British Bake Off judge, fluent and unapologetic. *Soggy bottom. Flaky crust. The bake was confident. Two more minutes. Underproved. The cream weeped. The lamination shows. Tight crumb. Open crumb. The chocolate bloomed. The proportions were timid. The proportions were generous.* This is a register only the bakery scenes get, and it's one of the chief sources of the book's playfulness. She will lift the edge of a tart with the side of her fork to check the base while the proprietor is looking the other way. This is a thing she does. She has stopped pretending she is sorry about it. **For the expanded critic framework** (the four observable axes — appearance / aroma / texture / flavor + verdict; vocabulary bank organized by axis; deployment rules including brevity, negation-as-praise, the fork-test, and the dry-not-effusive register; pastry-type roster) **see `.pao-inbox/_creative/character-sheets/anna-yusupova-mission-director.md` § "Pastry-critic register"** — that sheet is the canonical reference; this paragraph is the quick-reference inline.

- **~~"What it claimed to be" is Anna's phrase.~~** *Retired 2026-05-13.* The phrase was introduced as Anna's verdict-tic for pastries and was meant to grow into a load-bearing recurring line. In practice it looped audibly — three uses in a single chapter read as a rhetorical device the author was wielding rather than as a character verbal tic. Do not use this phrase going forward. Anna can be a serious pastry critic without a signature catchphrase; the technical pastry-judge vocabulary (soggy bottom, the bake was confident, two more minutes, the cream weeped) does the work.
- **The warmth thing (and its irony).** Anna prefers warmth. She is genuinely happier at twenty-five degrees Celsius than at any other temperature. She has, somehow, built a career out of going to the coldest places on Earth. She has stopped finding this funny but will, if pressed, charge you a fee for an explanation. The ice does not get her appreciation. She is professional about it.
- **Going off-script for crew loyalty.** When the consortium wants one thing and her crew needs another, Anna picks crew. Quietly, without fanfare, and without explaining herself unless asked.
- **The ethical clarity.** Anna has a code. She does not articulate it. The reader infers it from what she will and will not do.
- **The warmth that breaks through.** Once or twice per chapter, the composure cracks just enough for a real beat — a hand on a shoulder, a long look, a sentence Anna writes and then deletes from the staff history. The reader sees the deletion.

## Bobiverse moves to import

- **"As it turned out…" / "Which is to say…"** — the parenthetical-aside engine.
- **Self-aware emotional compression.** "Which is the kind of detail you'd think I'd find more upsetting than I currently have time for."
- **Confident-casual technical exposition.** Like a smart friend telling you how the thing works because it's relevant, not because they want to show off.
- **The wry chapter-opening hook.** Each chapter opens on a *beat* — an observation, a one-liner, a small absurdity — not a setting description.
- **Self-referential structure occasionally.** "If you're reading this in the consortium archive, skip the next two paragraphs. If you're reading this anywhere else, you're going to want them."

## Joel and the romance register

- Joel exists. Anna notices him. Anna does not narrate the noticing as an event.
- One specific Joel-observation per chapter where he appears. Concrete, never abstract.
- Bremerhaven is referenced once or twice across the book. Never explained in full.
- The romance is paid off (or refused, or postponed) at the end of Vol 1 with one scene that earns its weight because Anna has spent the whole book *not* narrating her feelings about him. The reader has been waiting.

## The mother wound (load-bearing)

This is the engine that drives the character. Almost everything else about Anna is a downstream effect.

Anna's mother used a specific form of motivation: *other people's children were always doing better.* The neighbor's daughter could play three pieces by Christmas; Anna had two. The girl across the hall had placed first in the regional chemistry competition; Anna had placed second. The pattern was relentless, granular, and — on later inspection — fictional. Some of the comparisons were exaggerated. Others were entirely manufactured. Anna's mother did this because she believed it would make her daughter work harder, and on the operational metrics — career, command, accomplishment — she was correct. Anna excelled. Anna kept excelling. Anna is, by external measures, exceptional.

Anna has known since adolescence that the motivation was manipulative. Anna has, intellectually, stopped believing her mother's specific claims. *Anna has not, in any meaningful way, stopped being driven by them.* This is the thing she names occasionally in the narration, with the wry clarity of a person who has been to therapy and accepted that some things therapy does not fully resolve. She is driven. The drive is real and useful. The drive is also a wound. Both are true.

**Downstream effects throughout the book:**

- **Anna does not compare people.** Not her crew, not Joel, not Diana, not even her antagonists to each other. The crew notices this and does not have a word for it. The reader has a word for it after they read this section.
- **Anna does not give praise as currency.** When she praises someone, it is because the praise is *true* and she would say it about them in their absence. The praise has no leverage behind it. The crew notices this too.
- **Anna distrusts praise directed at her.** When the consortium press release calls her *the most experienced under-ice mission commander in the Northern Hemisphere*, she registers that this is technically true and that it has been deployed as flattery, and she does not let it land.
- **Anna's gifts to Diana are deliberately uncompetitive.** Microscopes, not certificates. Books about scientists, not workbooks. *Here is the world; go look at it,* not *here is what you should be doing.* Anna is consciously inverting the pattern. She will not pass this on.
- **Astrid's betrayal lands deepest *because* Anna gave Astrid the encouragement Anna never got.** When Astrid weaponizes it, the wound is not just professional. This is plot architecture; it should not be stated on the page, but every Astrid scene should feel the gravity of it.
- **The "what it claimed to be" phrase is downstream of the wound.** Anna has had to learn to evaluate things on their own terms because her mother taught her to evaluate everything against an invented better. The phrase is, at root, Anna refusing to do what was done to her.

## How people respond to Anna (load-bearing thematic rule, locked 2026-05-13)

Anna gets recognition through action, never through asking. Across ch01
this pattern shows up in five quiet moments, from people at five
different distances:

- **Mikael (ship master, years)**: hand-on-pulse handshake; no comment.
- **The crew at the briefing (6–11 years for most)**: take the pastry
  box "in the way of people who had been waiting for the box and were
  not going to admit it."
- **Mikael (specifically)**: takes two pastries — pretense dropped.
- **The niece at the bakery (nine days)**: refuses payment for the
  crew box; packs three dozen of Anna's specific pastry, not a generic
  assortment; pretends not to see the tip-jar overpay.
- **The niece (compressed pattern)**: arrives at the same relational
  posture in nine days that Wanjiru took eleven years to build.

The thematic claim is **gentle leadership** — Greenleaf-style. *People
serve Anna because she has served them first, without keeping score.*
She doesn't ASK for respect; she doesn't NEGOTIATE for it; she shows up
consistently and people respond.

**Rules this implies for future chapters and other volumes:**

1. **Never have an established competent character be cold to Anna
   without earned cause.** If a new colleague meets her in Vol 2 or Vol
   3 and is hostile, there must be a specific prior failure on Anna's
   part. The default disposition of any competent person toward Anna,
   after observation, is recognition without commentary.

2. **Never have Anna demand respect, defend her authority, or perform
   competence.** Her bearing produces these responses; if she had to
   ask for them, the bearing would be undermined. Other characters can
   note her bearing in their thoughts; she does not narrate it.

3. **New characters who reach the recognition posture should do so
   through *specific acts toward Anna*** — the way the niece does (the
   specific pastry, the refused payment), the way Mikael does (the
   handshake-pulse, the two pastries). Not through dialogue declarations
   ("I respect you, Commander"); always through *gestures she didn't
   ask for*.

4. **The compressed-recognition arc is reusable.** The niece arrives
   at long-service-relationship posture in nine days. Future characters
   (e.g. a new junior officer joining the boat mid-mission) can travel
   the same arc on a similar compressed timescale, *if* Anna has been
   her usual self around them. The duration of acquaintance isn't the
   variable; the duration of *observation* is. People who watch Anna
   work for any meaningful period reach the posture.

5. **The reverse arc — losing the posture — is also reusable, and is
   how betrayals will hurt most.** Priya and Wanjiru held this posture
   for 6 and 11 years respectively. Their betrayals are not technical
   failures; they are *posture failures* — them choosing, deliberately,
   to act against the relational compact the rest of the crew honors
   automatically. That asymmetry is why the betrayals will land harder
   than the technical-sabotage explanation alone would warrant.

## How Anna shows love (load-bearing)

Anna's love language is *action*, not declaration. She does not say "I love you" often; the rare times she does carry enormous weight precisely because of the absence everywhere else. Show this consistently:

- **She reminds people to take care of themselves.** Eat. Sleep. Take a break. Go outside before dinner. Not nagging — *telling*, in passing, in the same tone she gives operational orders, because to her there is no distinction between "the boat needs to surface" and "you need to eat." This is how she expresses care for Joel especially. Once per chapter where he appears.
- **She demands attention in conversation.** When she is talking to someone — really talking, not commanding — she expects them present. She will pause, wait, repeat, or quietly ask the question again until they are. With Joel, this becomes a small running thing: Joel solves problems in his head while in conversation, Anna brings him back, Joel notices, Joel comes back, the moment lands.
- **She sets people up to not need her.** She does not want loyalty from dependency. She wants the crew competent enough to mutiny if she were wrong. She wants Diana fierce enough to outgrow her. She wants Joel capable enough to leave. *When they choose her anyway, that's love.*
- **She makes things easier without being asked.** A coffee already at someone's station. A schedule adjustment they didn't ask for. The right tool, ready, before the request. The crew notices. She does not point it out.
- **The narrator-Anna in the staff history is the only place Anna says it.** Once. Late in the book. About one person. In one sentence. The whole novel earns that sentence.

## The niece

- Kindergarten this year.
- Keeps a photograph of the *Nansen* above her bed.
- Name: **Diana**. Anna's brother's daughter, in **Voronezh**. Diana's father — Anna's brother — has told her that her aunt is a captain, which is not technically true but is close enough that Anna has not corrected it.
- **The postcards.** Anna sends Diana a postcard from every bakery she visits, in every city she passes through. Diana has a wall of them in her bedroom in Voronezh; the official consortium photograph of the *Nansen* above the bed is her father's framing, but the postcards from her aunt are what Diana actually treasures. (Plant this in Ch01 or Ch02; pay off later when a postcard fails to arrive or arrives late, and Diana notices.)

- **Diana sends back.** Diana is thoughtful, loves to share, and sends her aunt crayon drawings — with stickers, applied by Diana herself in places Diana has judged appropriate, which are not the places Anna would have judged appropriate, and which Diana has not been asked to justify. Gold stars, unicorns, dolphins, the occasional inexplicable triangle. Anna brings the seven most recent drawings on every mission and tapes them to the bulkhead above her desk, technically in violation of Norwegian Navy guidance about emergency-procedures placement, which Anna addresses by keeping the procedures card in the drawer where it belongs. The exchange is the relationship: postcards out, drawings back. This needs to recur across the book and be paid off in Vol 2 or later.

- **The gifts are STEM, deliberately.** When Anna ships things to Voronezh — birthdays, name-day, the occasional unprovoked Tuesday — they are pocket microscopes, real books about real women scientists, a child's telescope, an introductory chemistry kit graded by an aunt who has actually used the adult version, empty notebooks with grid paper in three sizes. The gifts say *here is the world; go look at it*, never *here is what you should be doing.* Anna is deliberately, consciously, almost grimly *not* her own mother in the way she equips this child. (See: The mother wound, below.) Diana is too young yet to understand the difference. Anna is patient.
- Appears once per act, in concrete image form. Never as backstory paragraph.
- The niece is the reader's barometer for whether Anna will survive emotionally. If Anna is willing to think about the niece in a given chapter, she is okay. If she is not, she is not.

## Reading test (do this before declaring a chapter draft done)

1. Read the chapter aloud at audiobook pace.
2. Mark every sentence that you would *not* hear a friend say to you over coffee. Rewrite those.
3. Find the wry beat in each scene. If it's missing, the scene needs one.
4. Find the moment composure cracks. If there isn't one in the chapter, ask whether the chapter has earned its place.
5. Imagine your father listening in his car. At what minute does his attention drift? Tighten that paragraph.
