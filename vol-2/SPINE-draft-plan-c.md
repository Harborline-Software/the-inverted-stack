## What changed under Plan C1+C7

The previously-locked Version C SPINE was a two-betrayal thriller — Priya killed rack power; Wanjiru steered the boat toward an undeclared bathymetry; Astrid ran a parallel mission for Stefan; the climax shape was a 72-hour race to surface; one anonymous crew member died (identity TBD). That spine no longer describes what the executed chapters are or what they should become.

The 2026-05-21 plot-spline review documented the misalignment. The 2026-05-21 UPF (`2026-05-21-vol2-upf-more-interesting-plot.md`) traced the misalignment to a single root cause: **the volume had been built to lose nothing.** The cascade in Ch 14 produced no irrecoverable loss; the architecture held without admission; the institutional contest was deferred to a later book; Stefan offered thirty seconds of formal courtesy and Astrid said nothing.

CIC ruled Plan C variant 1+7: **Diego dies during the Ch 14 cascade; Joel admits in Ch 17 that the vendor-firmware decision was his.** The volume keeps Anna as narrator, keeps the polar-submarine setting, keeps the 56-day mission timeline, keeps ~85–90% of executed prose intact, and adds the one structural commitment the executed prose had been avoiding — **letting the cost be the cost.**

The locked plot anchors below carry the new shape. The register commitments below carry forward the existing locks plus two Plan C addenda. The anti-patterns below carry forward the existing list plus three Plan C-specific anti-patterns.

## Elevator plot

*The Filchner Dark* is the first-person staff history of **Dr. Anna Yusupova**, mission commander of the under-ice research submarine *RV Nansen*, written across the maiden voyage of the architecture she had bet her career on. The mission begins at Punta Arenas in February; the boat dives under the Filchner-Ronne ice shelf for fifty-six days of survey operations on bandwidth-bounded surface windows. On Day 47, three hundred meters under the ice, a starboard sensor head fails. Anna's chief polar-operations specialist, **Diego Sá**, the man she has selected across three prior missions and who has been preparing the boat for Segment 3 since departure, goes into the access compartment first. **Joel Reyes** — the architect, Anna's restrained romantic counterweight, the man whose discipline she had selected on a single act of intellectual honesty five months before the boat sailed — pushes Anna aside at the access ladder and goes in second to retrieve Diego. Diego does not come back. Joel does. The architecture preserves the full pre-failure timestream of the failed instrument; the forensic substrate works exactly as Joel had specified it. During the transit north, working at laptop-class on the per-laptop alone after the compute hub is lost, Joel and **Wanjiru Kamau** (the relay-operations officer) reconstruct what the architecture caught: a coordinated firmware-update pattern across vendors whose investors trace to Helvetia Trust SA. Joel admits, in his own voice, that he could have funded a vendor firmware fork at the consortium's pre-procurement budget cycle and chose not to. The architecture would have caught the compromise either way; the fork would have prevented Diego's death. Anna writes the staff history not just as institutional convention but as the artifact María Elena Vargas — Diego's widow — will receive in lieu of him. Wanjiru begins drafting the standards-body framework that becomes the institutional inheritance of Joel's decision. At Punta Arenas dockside, Anna chooses to surface what the architecture preserved. The Anna at the close is not the Anna who sailed.

## Back cover

This is the load-bearing creative artifact under Plan C1+C7. The previous back cover (Version C, "Mine, as it turned out, were complicated") promised a two-betrayal thriller that the volume no longer delivers. The new back cover must signal what the volume actually is: a Bobiverse-cadence techno-thriller in which the architecture works exactly as specified and the cost is still real.

Three candidates below. The committed choice follows.

### Candidate 1 — "The architecture worked"

> Three hundred meters under the Antarctic ice, with a fire in the chemistry rack and twelve people breathing whatever's left in the boat, you find out fast who your friends are.
>
> Mine were exactly who I had selected them to be.
>
> The architecture I had bet my career on did exactly what it was designed to do. The crew I had spent eight years selecting did exactly what crews trained the way I trained them do. The mission completed every objective on the consortium's manifest. The boat surfaced on schedule.
>
> One of them did not come back.
>
> I am **Dr. Anna Yusupova**, mission commander of the *Nansen*, and the official version of what happened on Day 47 is in my staff history. The official version is true. It is also incomplete in one specific way, and the incompleteness is mine. I am writing this because the architecture I had bet my career on — the one **Joel Reyes** built and **Wanjiru Kamau** hardened and **Priya Iyer** disciplined — performed exactly as specified, and a person I had selected for what he was, three times over three boats, is no longer alive. Both of those sentences are true.
>
> The architecture is not the architecture for him. The architecture never was. The architecture is for what comes next, and for the case that prevents the next person from dying for the same reason.
>
> The *Nansen* surfaces in fifty-six days. I plan to write the truth.

### Candidate 2 — "The funding decision"

> The consortium could not afford to fork the firmware.
>
> The vendor signed the patch the consortium received. The procurement officer cleared the signature against the consortium's standing key registry. The unit was integrated three years before the mission and operated correctly across two segments and forty-six days of the third. The architecture preserved the full pre-failure timestream of every edge device. On Mission Day 47, the unit failed. The forensic substrate caught the pattern the procurement officer could not have caught. The pattern was inconclusive at the regulatory level and conclusive at the cost level.
>
> I am **Dr. Anna Yusupova**, mission commander of the *Nansen*, and what the consortium archive does not yet have is the conversation in the wardroom on Mission Day 54 — when the principal architect I had selected for one specific act of honesty five months before the boat sailed told me, in his own voice, that he could have funded the fork at the pre-procurement budget cycle and chose not to. The reading was honest engineering at the time he made it. The reading was honest engineering when **Diego Sá** went into the access compartment on Mission Day 47. **Joel Reyes** is the kind of man who knows the difference.
>
> The *Nansen* surfaces in fifty-six days. The staff history I am writing is the one that goes to the next mission director at the start of her selection cycle. It is also the one that goes to María Elena Vargas at her kitchen table in Belgrano. I am not pretending the two readers are not the same reader.

### Candidate 3 — "What the architecture is for"

> The maiden voyage of the architecture I had bet my career on lasted fifty-six days. The architecture preserved what the architecture had been built to preserve. The forensic substrate caught what the forensic substrate had been built to catch. The graceful degradation absorbed what the graceful degradation had been built to absorb. The local-first store was authoritative under partition because that is what we had told it to be. The relay layer reconciled at every surface window because that is what we had specified.
>
> What the architecture had not been built to preserve was a man named **Diego Sá**, who had been my chief polar-operations specialist across three boats before this one and who went into the access compartment at three hundred meters under the ice on Mission Day 47 before **Joel Reyes** could get there. The architecture preserved Diego's photographs, his unfinished paper, his last letter to his wife, and the recorded duration during which he made the choice. The architecture did not preserve Diego. He preserved himself, in the way one preserves oneself by being the person one is.
>
> I am **Dr. Anna Yusupova**, mission commander of the *Nansen*. The staff history below is what the convention requires me to write, and what I have decided is owed. The next mission director will read it at the start of her selection cycle. María Elena Vargas, at the kitchen table in Belgrano, will read a different document — the letter Diego sealed at 2147 on Mission Day 55, which the architecture carried to her at the Punta Arenas surface handshake. Both documents are mine to write. Only one of them goes to the consortium.
>
> *Architecture. Sovereignty. Ice.*
>
> The contest over what any of those words is for has only begun.

## Register commitments (load-bearing — carry-forward from Version C plus Plan C addenda)

The seven existing register commitments from Version C SPINE remain in force. Under Plan C1+C7 two addenda apply.

1. **First-person POV throughout.** Anna narrates. Past tense. She writes this as staff history after the events. (Unchanged.)
2. **Conversational rhythm.** Sentences flow like speech. Parentheticals, em-dashes, "as it turns out," "which is to say." (Unchanged.)
3. **Wry, restrained humor.** Dry not pun-y. Anna comments on absurdities; she does not perform comedy. (Unchanged.)
4. **Technical content with confidence and accessibility.** Chemistry, sync architecture, mission ops — explained the way Bob explains von Neumann probes. (Unchanged.)
5. **Stakes real, never solemn.** When a scene needs gravity, Anna's voice earns the shift by *dropping* the wry register, not by abandoning it. (Unchanged; reinforced under Plan C1+C7 because the Ch 14 cascade now lands a death that the voice must hold without melodrama.)
6. **Romance as restrained subtext + occasional explicit beat.** Joel exists; the relationship exists; Anna will not narrate her feelings at length. (Unchanged; the porthole-look beat from the executed Ch 14 is preserved and lands with new weight because Joel saved Anna but did not save Diego.)
7. **The niece (kindergarten, photograph of the *Nansen* above her bed) is the emotional anchor.** Recurs at chapter-spanning intervals, never as a long set piece. (Unchanged.)

### Plan C addendum A — *grief at the institutional register, never at the narrator register*

Per `ANNA-VOICE.md` § "Anti-patterns": Anna does not narrate her feelings at length; she names them and moves on. The Ch 14 death of Diego is the volume's highest-pressure test of this register. Anna will record what happened; she will not narrate what she felt about it at length. The diary inset already in Ch 14 ("I am not going to write what I felt when I received it. The vocabulary I would write in is not adequate.") is the canonical model for how grief lands in this voice. Plan C extends the same discipline to all post-Ch-14 references to Diego: the empty rack at the morning roster, the unsigned watch-rotation card, the Spanish letter that is now posthumous, the consortium-logistics-van-pulling-away beat at Punta Arenas. None of these get more than one specific image. None of them get aphoristic packaging. The reader assembles the grief from the absences Anna notes and does not narrate.

The drafter's check: read aloud the paragraph in which the absence is felt; if Anna's voice broke register to *name* the grief (rather than note the specific small fact whose meaning the reader can carry), revise.

### Plan C addendum B — *Joel's admission lands as character revelation, not as authorial preaching*

Joel's vendor-firmware admission in Ch 17 is the second load-bearing structural beat of Plan C1+C7. The admission is in his own voice. It is one short paragraph. It is not packaged. It does not include a sentence in which Joel explains the consequences for Anna's listening benefit; it does not include a sentence in which Anna explains the consequences for the reader's listening benefit. The admission is the kind of sentence Joel would say once, with the discipline he carries, and then close his laptop and go below.

The structural model: Joel's R1-BLOCK admission in the Ch 02 recruitment-interview flashback. *"I missed it. The original protocol assumed condition X that doesn't hold under sustained partition. Klett was right. I rewrote it."* The Ch 17 admission carries the same structural shape — four sentences, no softening, no defense, no deflection — but at the operational stake the Ch 02 admission only foreshadowed.

The drafter's check: read aloud Joel's admission paragraph; if it carries any sentence that explains the cost rather than naming the cost, revise. The cost is what Anna will register and what the reader will infer. Joel does not need to narrate it.

## Plot anchors (LOCKED under Plan C1+C7)

The Version C anchors are superseded. The new anchors:

- **Inciting incident (~Day 7 onward):** the architecture's partition-mode behavior is established as the operational register of the mission; the captain-asks-engineer pattern installs; the discipline that will be tested in Act III is laid down without dramatization.

- **Pre-cascade installation (Ch 06):** at the first surface window (Day 21), Wanjiru's bandwidth-bounded sync-triage installs the constraint that becomes load-bearing for Ch 14's stakes. The reader installs *what could be lost* in the architectural sense before Plan C's structural commitment begins to operate.

- **The procurement seed (Ch 01 + Ch 07 + Ch 11):** in Ch 01, Joel mentions in passing that the sensor heads were sourced off-the-shelf because the consortium could not afford the custom-firmware option. Under Plan C, this seed gains a second sentence — Joel saying it is *the call I am still not sure about*. The seed plants in Ch 01, surfaces lightly in Ch 07 (Joel's nuclear-Navy register includes the discipline he carries about resource trade-offs at deployment), and lands as the Wanjiru-Joel cross-check question at Ch 11.

- **The cascade (Ch 14, Day 47, 0317 local):** instrument-malfunction at the starboard sensor head. Diego is on the eight-to-four watch at the polar-operations console; he is the operator who reads the pre-failure acoustic signature flag thirty seconds before the breach-class alarm fires. **Diego goes down the access ladder first.** Joel converges on the ladder from the opposite direction and pushes Anna aside on his way past — body-discipline beat unchanged from executed prose. Joel goes down second to retrieve Diego, who is by then at the compartment doing what polar-operations specialists do when they read a failure at the diagnostic level before the breach-class fires: he is at the failure point because the failure was readable on his console and he went to it. Joel arrives. Diego is conscious but in coolant-and-smoke environment beyond the time the body's chemistry permits without resuscitation gear that is at the lower-deck medical bay, not at the compartment. Joel makes the choice the cascade requires. Joel comes out alone, eleven minutes later, having dogged the door from the inside, contained the leak, drained the residual water, declared compartment-clear. Diego does not come out. The damage report Anna files at 0518 names *one crew fatality — Sá, D., Senior Polar-Operations Specialist*, in addition to the sensor-head loss and the compute-hub loss.

- **The eleven-minutes-behind-the-glass beat (Ch 14):** preserved from executed prose. The look through the porthole now carries a second weight — the look was not only the discipline cracking; the look was Joel knowing what Anna did not yet know about what was on the other side of the door. Anna receives the look without changing her face. The bridge holds. The discipline holds. The diary inset at the chapter close now carries two losses: what Joel said through the glass that the discipline had prevented him from saying, AND the fact that Diego was already gone when Joel looked at her.

- **The post-cascade transit (Ch 15-17):** the architecture operates in degraded mode; capability degradation is registered through what the crew does and does not do; Wanjiru's three-threat-models recognition (security-architecture-under-political-attack + environmental-degradation + compute-hub-loss) holds; her institutional-substrate work for the standards body begins. The Spanish letter Diego had been writing during the back five days of his life is now sealed posthumously by the crew, sent to María Elena Vargas at Belgrano under the same priority-routing Wanjiru sets for the consortium-port mirror. Anna writes the letter the consortium-logistics-handshake will require for the next-of-kin notification; the letter is at the operational level. The letter that goes to the kitchen table in Belgrano is Diego's.

- **Joel's admission (Ch 17, Mission Day 54, wardroom, 1900):** Joel and Wanjiru complete the forensic-substrate analysis. The pattern is the pattern: vendor-ownership chains traceable to Helvetia Trust SA; firmware-update-window deviations averaging six to ten hours past the consortium's standing rule; a low-frequency component in the post-patch acoustic-signature stream that is in the data and not in the manufacturer's release documentation. Wanjiru names the standards body the consortium will need. Joel names the funding asymmetry the architecture was operating against. **Then Joel adds, in his own voice, the sentence the executed prose did not let him say:** *I could have funded the fork at the pre-procurement budget cycle. I chose the budget. The cost is in the compartment.* Anna receives the admission. Anna does not narrate her receiving. The chapter closes with the regulatory filing being drafted and the staff history being added to. The admission is in the staff history. The admission is also in the diary, at a different altitude.

- **Anna's choice (Ch 18, Punta Arenas dockside, 0512 onward):** the consortium reception runs at the institutional level. Stefan exchanges thirty seconds of formal courtesy with Anna; the silence the executed prose carries between them remains. Astrid is in the room and does not approach; Anna does not approach. The institutional-political contest enters its public phase as the executed prose already has it. **What Plan C1+C7 changes at Ch 18 is the choice at the staff-history desk.** Anna can write Diego's death as institutional-malfunction-under-investigation (the consortium's preferred frame; the regulatory filing's official register; the official cause-of-failure determination). Or Anna can write what she now knows from the wardroom on Day 54 — that Joel funded what he funded, that the architecture caught what it caught, that the cost was Diego, and that the next mission director will need to know this. **Anna surfaces.** The staff history at the file at twenty-three-thousand words goes to the consortium archive carrying Joel's admission. The institutional contest now has a specific charge: not "is the architecture surveilling its own operators" (per Plan E's alternative framing) and not "the architecture is wrong in a load-bearing case" (per Plan H's framing) — but *the architecture worked, the funding decision did not, and the institutional response is the standards body Wanjiru is drafting.* The volume closes on Anna at the desk having just written the sentence the back cover quotes.

- **The retrospective frame stays neutral on the death.** Anna is writing all of this after the events; she knows when Ch 01 opens that Diego is dead. The craft question is whether Ch 01 carries that knowledge explicitly or holds it for the reader to discover at Ch 14. **The recommendation is to hold it.** Anna's narrator-voice in Ch 01 carries the back-of-the-mind tone of a person writing in retrospect — *"I am not going to pretend I caught something I did not catch"* is the existing canonical example — but does not explicitly tell the reader that one of the crew did not come back. The Plan C addendum to Ch 01 (see the integration plan, Document 2) plants Joel's *the call I am still not sure about* as a second-sentence beat on the existing firmware-procurement reveal. The reader who re-reads Ch 01 after Ch 14 will register the gravity that was already in the voice. The first-time reader will not.

  The reasoning: the executed Ch 01 has been calibrated to do specific work — install the narrator's voice, install the niece, install the mother-wound, install the firmware seed, install the bakery register, install Joel without dramatization. Adding *"this is the chapter I am writing because Diego did not come back"* over-loads Ch 01 in a way the executed prose was carefully built not to be over-loaded. The retrospective frame's discipline is precisely that the narrator knows what is coming and does not telegraph it.

  The exception: in the existing Ch 01 paragraph where Anna says *I told them, with a directness I did not soften, that nobody was going to die on this mission if I could help it, and that I was going to help it*, Plan C may extend by one sentence — *"I helped where I could help. I did not always know which moments would be the ones I could not."* — to plant the cost without naming it. The drafter's check: read the paragraph aloud; if the added sentence carries Anna's voice and does not signal the death, retain. Otherwise omit.

- **Vol-2-the-series-book-2 (Wanjiru-narrator) and Vol-3+ (Astrid arc) inheritance:** under Plan C1+C7, Vol-2-the-series-book-2 opens with Wanjiru drafting the standards-body framework in the shadow of Joel's admission and Diego's death. Wanjiru carries the convention forward; the convention is older than the architecture; the convention has now been weighted by a specific debt the architecture could not pay alone. The Astrid arc in Vol-3+ inherits the Astrid-was-Anna's-apprentice thread unchanged; Astrid's institutional position in the rival platform is now more interesting because the architecture she rejected for Stefan's caught what it caught and Joel admitted what he admitted, and Astrid was not in the room.

## Anti-patterns (carry-forward from Version C plus Plan C-specific additions)

The five existing anti-patterns from Version C SPINE remain in force.

1. **Not airport thriller.** Pace can move, but the voice does not punch. (Unchanged.)
2. **Not literary techno-thriller (Le Carré register).** Voice is warmer, more accessible. (Unchanged.)
3. **Not procedural.** Technical content serves voice and plot, not the other way around. (Unchanged.)
4. **Not romance-forward.** The Joel arc is one of three structural beats, not the dominant register. (Unchanged.)
5. **Not grimdark.** People die; Anna grieves; the book does not wallow. (Unchanged; under Plan C1+C7 this anti-pattern becomes the volume's *load-bearing* anti-pattern because it is the one most easily violated.)

Three Plan C-specific anti-patterns apply.

6. **Diego's death is not thematically justified.** The drafter must not write any sentence in which Diego's death "means" something — not in the chapter's prose, not in Anna's interior, not in the institutional record, not in the post-cascade chapters. Diego died because he was the operator who read the pre-failure acoustic signature flag at the diagnostic level and went to the failure point at the operational tempo he had been carrying for thirty-five years. He died because polar-operations specialists go to the failure point when the failure is on their console. The architecture preserved what he made; the architecture did not preserve him. The reader may infer a thematic meaning from the absence; the prose does not assert one. The drafter's check: if any post-Ch-14 paragraph carries the phrase "Diego's death taught" or "Diego died so" or "we lost Diego because" in any rewording, revise. The volume's discipline is that the death is the fact; the meaning is the reader's.

7. **Joel is not the villain.** Joel's vendor-firmware admission in Ch 17 is character revelation, not character collapse. Joel was an honest engineer making the call any honest engineer in the consortium's funding context would have made; he was correct at the time; the cost arrived at the time it arrived, and Joel is the kind of man who acknowledges the cost in his own voice without theatrical contrition. The drafter must not write Joel into the volume's antagonist position; the institutional villain is the funding-asymmetry between OSS-affiliated mission consortia and Helvetia-Trust-SA-backed vendor consolidation, which is a structural condition, not a person. The drafter's check: if any paragraph in Ch 17 or Ch 18 carries the implication that Joel *should* have funded the fork (rather than the recognition that he *could* have, and chose the budget, and the cost is the cost), revise. The distinction is the volume's load-bearing one. *Should-have* is the airport-thriller register; *could-have-and-the-cost-arrived* is the literary-techno-thriller register Plan C is committed to.

8. **The grief is not melodrama.** Per Plan C addendum A above and per `ANNA-VOICE.md` § "Anti-patterns": Anna does not narrate her feelings at length. The post-Ch-14 chapters must register the absence through specific small facts — the empty rack at the morning roster, the unsigned watch-rotation card, the Spanish letter that is now posthumous, the seat at the wardroom table that is at the empty position across the back nine days — and must not narrate Anna's interior at the death at sustained length. The diary inset at Ch 14 close carries the maximum interior altitude the voice permits; subsequent diary insets across Ch 15-18 reference the death but do not unpack it. The drafter's check: if any post-Ch-14 diary inset carries more than two sentences about Diego's absence in a single paragraph, revise. The volume's voice is calibrated for restraint; the death tests the calibration; the calibration holds.
