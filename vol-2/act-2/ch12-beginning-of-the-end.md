*Crew log, 2026-10-27T09:12Z. RV Nansen, MERIDIAN-7. Yusupova, Mission Director, reporting Segment 3 dive commencement.*

*Segment 3 dive at 0612 local. The boat departed Surface 2 at 0500; transit-to-operational-depth completed at 0608; routine submerged ops at 0612. This is the last under-ice deployment of MERIDIAN-7. The final scheduled surface is at Punta Arenas on Day 56 - surfacing for transit, then docking. Ten days against the chart in Segment 3, against current routing. Segment 3 closes at the Drake Passage transit, not at a research-window surface. There is no Surface 3. Sync queue cleared at Surface 2 close per Wanjiru's end-of-window filing at 0500; carryover documented; bulk multimedia and remaining personal-author streams carry to final docking. Archive state attested before submersion at 0457 against the boat-internal redundancy layer; hash chain continuous since boat last surfaced. Crew at full station. Routine register.*

*- Filed to mission-archive; hash 4f02...c7d9*

The dive sequence ran eight minutes long against Diego's chart. The thermocline at one hundred and forty meters had stratified harder than the model said it would, which is the polite way of saying the model was wrong and the water was the water. Mikael took the eight minutes the boat needed. The trim corrected at 0608. The acoustic floor laid down at 0610 on the new bearing. The engine note settled to the low-speed ratio - the one that means the boat is settled and the boat knows it.

I came onto the bridge at 0611. Diego was at the polar-operations station to the right of Mikael, standing.

I will tell you, because Diego will not, that he had been on the bridge through the dive sequence - through the trim and the bearing and the thermocline read - because Segment 3 was the segment he had been preparing for since departure. The polar-operations slot was his slot now in a way it had not been in Segment 1 or Segment 2. The deeper bathymetry along the Filchner-Ronne approach was where his thirty-five years of polar oceanography sat at their densest. He stood at his console with his weight forward.

He turned when I came onto the bridge. He nodded.

He said: *trim correct. Bearing two-three-seven. Floor three hundred and twelve meters at the boat's mark; ice-edge two-point-one nautical miles north of forecast. The model is half a degree south on the edge again.*

I said: *acknowledged. Half a degree.*

He said: *I will run the back-correction against the consortium model when we have the next cross-section. Two hours.*

I said: *good.*

He turned back to his console. He did not settle into the chair. The polar-operations station runs four screens; he had three of them up at full and the fourth at the bathymetry feed at the higher refresh rate he had set at the start of the dive sequence. The console had been at the standard refresh through Segments 1 and 2. The refresh was up now because Diego was reading the bathymetry the way a man reads water he has read before and is no longer willing to trust to the boat's instinct alone.

Diego had not stood at the polar-operations console for an entire bridge watch in Segment 1 or Segment 2. He had taken the chair through the routine watches; he had stood for the partition transitions and the surface preparations and now, at 0611 on Day 39, for the commencement of Segment 3. He would stand through the first two hours of routine submerged ops. He would stand the next morning through the first cross-section against the consortium model. He would - I knew, because I had been on three previous Antarctic missions with him - stand more in Segment 3 than he had stood in Segments 1 and 2 combined. The bathymetry mattered now in a way it had not yet mattered. Diego's tempo was the tempo the boat was now operating at, and the rest of us would catch up to it on our own schedule.

I went to the command station. I read the routine indications.

I had a thought I did not write down. I will write it here.

*And now everything we record for the next ten days is on the boat alone until we surface in Punta Arenas.*

(Which is the kind of thing you know from departure day. The manifest says fifty-six days. The timeline shows three surface windows. Basic arithmetic. And then you are standing at the command station on Day 39 at 0611, and the body does the arithmetic again, and the arithmetic is the same, and the answer arrives at a different altitude than it did in September. The body had taken in Surface 1 as the surfacing it had been and Surface 2 as the surfacing it had been, and the body knew, as bodies on boats know, that there was not a third one. I noted that I had had the thought. I did not soften it.)

I returned to the indications. The watch ran. The bridge ran routine.

Joel came on watch in the engine room at 0600 against the standard rotation. He came up to the bridge at 0750 for the engineering handoff and stayed for the second half of the morning brief. Priya came onto the bridge at 0918 with the schema-state-check carryover from the segment boundary.

She had run the boundary check at 0512, against the local store, as the surface window was closing. First pass at 0512, second at 0524, third at 0537 - the protocol's three-pass requirement, in the cadence she walks it in when she is not in a hurry and there is nobody at her shoulder. The first two passes cleared. The third, at 0537, flagged an anomaly at one of the instrumentation streams against the migration-version handshake. The read-confirmation cycle caught it inside the third pass and rolled the affected entry back to the prior canonical schema before it committed to the segment-boundary write. The protocol had behaved the way the council had spent three months arguing it should behave. Priya had filed the catch at 0539, the rolled-back entry separately at 0541, her own three-pass-discipline note at 0543, and had carried the three filings to the morning brief at 0830 - where she had reported them in the clipped cadence she uses for things that have already been handled. *Three passes. One anomaly caught at three; rolled back; not propagated; no carry.*

She had not elaborated at the brief.

She had elaborated now.

She came onto the bridge with the laptop and a single sheet of paper. The paper was the rolled-back entry's metadata. She set the paper down on the chart console at the side of the bridge. She turned the laptop. The screen showed the three passes side by side - the first two in standard grey, the third in standard grey with the anomaly highlight on the affected entry, the rolled-back state below, the canonical schema referent above. Her shoulders were set the way her shoulders set when she is working a problem she does not yet have the shape of, against a discipline that does.

She said: *Director.*

I said: *the anomaly.*

She said: *one entry. Auxiliary salinity stream from the starboard probe. Schema-version field reported as v3.4.2; canonical at the segment boundary is v3.4.1. The handshake against the migration-version registry returned the catch on the third pass. The anomaly was the entry. The entry rolled back at the read-confirmation. Not propagated. Not committed. Not in Segment 2's canonical record. The check at the segment boundary is what the check is for.*

I said: *cause.*

She said: *unknown at this read. The starboard probe's firmware came up clean against the consortium-pushed manifest at oh-six-fourteen; that's Joel's record from the brief. The schema-version field on the auxiliary salinity stream is set at the firmware level, not at the application level, so the v3.4.2 read is what the firmware reported. The mismatch could be a manifest-level field carry that did not propagate to the schema-version registry at the consortium side; could be a local-clock skew at the probe between firmware-update commit and stream resumption; could be a single-frame artifact at the boundary read that will not recur. I ran the third pass at the contraction-phase tolerance the protocol requires and caught it at the read-confirmation; the architecture did what the architecture is supposed to do.*

I said: *the carry.*

She said: *no carry. The anomaly is in the audit log as a caught-and-rolled-back at oh-five-thirty-seven; the canonical record at the segment boundary is v3.4.1 across the auxiliary salinity stream and across all other streams that flow through the migration-version registry; the boundary write is clean. I will run a four-pass cycle at the next routine schema check at oh-twelve-hundred to confirm the v3.4.2 was not a recurring field carry. If the four-pass cycle clears, the anomaly closes against the audit log entry. If the four-pass cycle catches it again, I escalate.*

I said: *acknowledged. Four-pass at twelve hundred. Escalation if it catches.*

She said: *acknowledged.*

She picked up the paper from the chart console. She closed the laptop. She did not soften the reading on the way out. She did not say *the architecture held*. She did not say *the discipline caught what the discipline catches*. She said, at the door of the bridge, the one-word acknowledgment she had said at the beginning. *Acknowledged.* She left.

I noted the exchange. The segment boundary was clean at the canonical record. Priya had caught what the third pass had been engineered to catch, logged it, and named the four-pass she would run against the recurrence. She had not, at any point in the exchange, said the word *worry*. (Which is the closest Priya gets to telling you something is worth your attention - she names the next test, at the cadence at which it will run, and the absence of further commentary is the commentary.)

Joel was in the engineering compartment at 1218, at the auxiliary plant station, walking the second-watch handoff with one of the junior engineers - a graduate of the IIT-Delhi program Priya had recommended as a Harborline Shipyard community contributor four years before the mission. The junior was on his second engineering watch of the segment. Joel was walking him through the post-dive plant indications at the level the junior needed, and was not reaching past it.

I came into the compartment at the door. I did not interrupt. I stood there for the closing two minutes of the handoff.

Joel was reading the gauge in front of him. He was naming what the gauge reported and what the redundant indication on the secondary panel reported against the gauge. *Primary loop pressure two hundred and fourteen at the gauge. Secondary indication two hundred and fourteen at the panel. Match within the redundant-indications tolerance. Gauge to be read against the panel at every routine half-watch. If the gauge reads above two-twenty-eight or below one-ninety-eight against a matching panel, you escalate to the engineer of the watch. If the gauge reads above or below against a non-matching panel, you escalate immediately and isolate the loop at the secondary valve. You do not assume the gauge. You do not assume the panel. You read both.*

The junior nodded. He repeated the procedure in his own words. He was almost right. Joel corrected the *almost*. The junior repeated it again. He was right. Joel did not say *good*. Joel said *yes*. They moved to the next station.

I had heard the shape of that procedure before - in the thirty-five years of polar oceanography I had been adjacent to, in the watch-handoff cadence of the two surface research vessels I had commanded before the *Nansen*. I had not heard it at this bearing. The bearing was older. It was the bearing of watch-standing on a boat that had been built to do something other than science, by people who had taught Joel that the gauge in front of you is the gauge in front of you and you do not bring the rest of your day to the reading.

He had told me the name's origin at a wardroom conversation in Segment 2 - at 2107 on the night of Day 28, against a cup of black tea and the wardroom emptying around us, at the bearing of a man who would rather you know than discover. He had said the architecture had been named after the boat. He had said the boat had been a submarine called *Sunfish*. He had said the submarine had been decommissioned in 1998. Then he had finished his tea.

(The year I finished my doctorate. I noted the coincidence. I did not say anything about it. There are some pieces of information that, once you have them, you put down on the wardroom table between you and do not pick up again until the other person does.)

The tightening was not the tightening of new-segment unease. It was the tightening of last-leg work, which is a tightening I have carried before, on other boats, and which has a known shape and a known cost. I noted it. I did not soften it. The boat had ten days.
