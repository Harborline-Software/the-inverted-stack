The Strait of Magellan was behind us by the second watch of Day 1. I came up through the Strait first as a junior, then as a chief scientist, and now as the woman in command, and I will tell you that the Strait is a kind of water you can love without effort. It is sheltered. It is glacial-fed. It is the grey-green you can stand at the rail of and read the depth of by eye. None of those things are true of what was waiting on the other side. It is not the Drake. The boat knew the difference before any of us did. Around eighteen hundred, the Nansen began to take the first of the swells the boat takes on the southern leg, and from that hour on the deck moved.

The first crossing was in 2006, on a Russian supply vessel out of Ushuaia — a junior science party member rotating to Bellingshausen and back. The boat was old. The crossing was rough. On the second night out, *rough* in the Drake's lexicon turned out to mean nothing the boat itself had not been built to absorb, and my own definition of rough — formed in the North Atlantic and on three Baltic crossings — was a definition the Drake did not honour. The word has not been used casually since. The Drake did not behave. It was not built to behave. The boat was built to take what the passage gave it and continue south. That was the contract.

The Nansen took it. She carried us through the night of Day 1, the morning of Day 2, and the long grey watch from Day 2 noon to Day 3 dawn the way a vessel of her class is built to: she pitched and she rolled and she did not, at any hour, sound like a boat in distress. The bridge officer of the morning watch on Day 3 told me the wind at oh-three-hundred had been forty-two knots gusting fifty-six. He told me with the flatness watch officers use for weather they do not intend to characterize. I noted his tone, logged the wind, and went below.

The thing the Drake does that the chart does not show is what it does to the period. A North Atlantic swell at fifty knots is honest weather; the period is long; the boat takes the long roll and gives you the second half of it back, and the body knows where the next pitch is coming from before the next pitch arrives. The Drake's period is shorter than that and is not regular. The boat takes one swell at one period and the next swell at another, and the third comes from a direction the second did not promise, because the wind that built the third was not the wind that built the second, and the Passage does not have anywhere for the wind to settle. The boat is reading three or four sea-states at once. The body that is on the boat is reading the boat that is reading the seas. Nothing in either reading is wrong; it is just that nothing is the same twice in a row, and the body has to keep paying attention.

The long afternoon of Day 2 went between the bridge and the wardroom. The standard things did not stay where the body had set them down. A mug carried from the wardroom to the bridge had to be carried with three points of contact - the hand at the cup, the hip at the bulkhead, the other hand on the rail - because the boat at twelve degrees of roll does not negotiate with a fluid in motion. The stairwell between decks took twice as long as the dry-dock stair took, and you used both hands going up, and you used one hand going down only because the rail rotated under the palm and the hand was correcting the rail's correction at the rate the rail was correcting. Joel had logged a quarter-inch of coffee on the engineering-compartment floor between his bunk and the rack, in his hand, with the cup held the way he held cups; this was Joel reporting that even his version of careful was not enough for the Drake at fifty knots, which was the only kind of report he had ever filed against himself in five months of working alongside him. I noted the coffee in the same notebook I noted everything else, and I did not mention it.

The southbound psychology was the thing I had to keep stepping over. The Drake is the southern continent's last unkindness before it lets you in - which is to say it is the southern continent reminding you that the continent does not need you to come, that the continent has been there without you for a long time, and that the only people the continent has any patience for are the ones who have learned to stop being surprised at the cost of being there. I have been south of the Drake enough times that the cost no longer surprises me. The not being surprised is a thing the body learns by being surprised the first three or four times, and is a thing one is required to perform in front of a junior crew member who is being surprised for the first time. There was no junior on the *Nansen* this trip. Everyone aboard had been south of the Drake at least twice. I did not have to perform the not-being-surprised for anyone. I performed it for myself, the way one continues to wear a shirt one has worn for long enough that the shirt no longer registers as a choice.

## The Paper and the Network — Sabina

Sabina was already at the wardroom desk when I came down. She had the consortium's port-call list out on the bench in two formats - a hard copy in the manila folder she carries everywhere and a tablet view in front of her - and she was reconciling items on each against a separate sheet of notes she had been keeping since an hour I had not been awake for. Her coffee was at her right hand and was not steaming. She looked up. She said *Director* in her working bearing - a person who had been on the work since before the watch began.

Across from her at the wardroom table, she walked me through what the consortium ports would expect from us. The first surface window at Day 21 - Helsinki ground station's relay schedule, signed in by AARI and the Geneva science office, Norwegian Polar Institute as the secondary contact, the Brazilian Antarctic program as the tertiary. The second surface window at Day 39 - the same primary, a different secondary, an additional consortium audit checkpoint the UAE-led leg of the consortium had asked for and that Sabina had pre-cleared with the AARI legal office before the boat had left port. The third window at Day 56, on approach to Punta Arenas - full propagation of the mission archive, the formal post-mission accounting, the medical roster's final state, the consortium's regulatory filings. She had hardcopies of the signoffs for all three.

Once, a year before the mission, the question of the duplicates came up. She said the network was not where the work lived. She said it the way one says a thing learned at a specific hour on a specific afternoon at a stage of one's life one does not now revisit. The question did not come up again. The hardcopies continued to be made; the hardcopies continued to travel; the manila folder sat at her right hand on the morning of Day 2 in the Drake.

Sabina did not finish that story that afternoon. She would not have. So I will. She came up through the Brazilian Antarctic program in the late 1990s, when the program was running on consortium funding that came with consortium telemetry, and she watched a junior officer at one of the partner programs lose six weeks of survey data because a network handoff between two ground stations had dropped a packet nobody at either station had been instructed to look for. The data was on hardcopy at both stations. The hardcopies had been filed correctly. Neither station's hardcopy had been consulted, because the network said the network had it. Sabina was twenty-eight in 1998 and decided, on the dock at Rio Grande while the junior officer wrote the loss report, that on every mission she ran after that one, the paper would be where the paper was. I have known her since 2011, and the paper has always been where the paper was. The folder at her right hand on the morning of Day 2 was not theatre. It was the practice of a woman who had been at a dock in 1998.

She said, midway through the second-window walkthrough: *the consortium will want the audit checkpoint to be clean.* She said it as a statement of operational fact, without commentary on whether the consortium's wanting it was reasonable or whether the UAE-led leg had pre-positioned the request for political reasons an officer who had spent twenty years reconciling consortium signoffs would have spotted. The wanting was the constraint. The cleanness was the work. She did not editorialize.

Logistics officers who editorialized have been a feature of every mission I have run. The editorializing is sometimes useful and is more often a tax the rest of the watch pays for the officer's positioning inside consortium politics. Sabina did not pay that tax. Sabina did the work. I logged it. I returned to the deck.

## The List at Low Altitude — Diego

Diego came up to the bridge cap at fourteen-twenty on Day 2 with a thermos he had brought from his cabin and the polar-operations chart he keeps on a folded paper that has been folded and unfolded enough times to have a topography of its own. He set the thermos down on the rail and the chart on the bench under the windscreen. He poured and gave me half. The coffee was the kind a man drinks when it is for the work and not for the pleasure of it. He has poured me coffee on boats before this one.

I asked him what the ice edge would look like at this season.

*The edge moves, Anna.* He said it the way a senior operator says a thing to another senior operator. *September is late winter for the southern continent; the edge has not retreated yet. We will reach it sooner than the consortium routing assumes. We will need to slow before the boat's master assumes we should.*

He tapped the chart at a point that was not where the consortium's pre-mission file had marked the expected ice edge. *The routing was drawn in Geneva. Geneva does not see the edge from the boat's wing. I have seen the edge from the boat's wing on this latitude in five Septembers. The edge is at this line, give or take a watch, depending on what the wind does.*

I asked him about the under-ice acoustic conditions on approach.

He paused. It was the pause of a man whose answer was a list and who was deciding which items belonged in the answer he gave the Mission Director on the wing on Day 2 and which belonged in the longer briefing he and I would both prefer at a later hour. *The marginal ice zone is loud. Open water under broken ice is louder than open water without ice; the brash-ice pieces grind against each other; the larger floes work against the swell underneath them. The acoustic floor on approach will not be clean. The boat's sonar will read the zone correctly because the sonar was designed to read it; the data instrumentation will pick up signal floor the consortium's pre-mission noise budget did not model in full. Priya will need to recalibrate before deployment. We can budget the time.*

He drank his coffee. The chart went back into its topography. He carried the thermos below.

What I had taken in, in Diego's two answers, was not the technical content. The content was correct; I would have been surprised if it had not been. The shape was the thing. Diego had a list of things that were going to be different at the ice edge from what the consortium had assumed, and he had been carrying the list at low altitude for the duration of the planning, and he had not surfaced it in the planning meetings because the meetings were not where the list belonged. The bridge cap on Day 2 was where the list belonged. He had brought it on schedule.

Polar specialists I had worked with carried more institutional rank than Diego, and performed expertise an order of magnitude louder. None of them had been quietly correct on the wing the way Diego had. Diego does not perform competence. Diego does the work. The performing is for the consortium meetings.

Before he went below he said Mikael had noted the same difference between the consortium routing and the actual edge but had not yet decided how to bring it up to the Mission Director. Diego was bringing it up first. He said it the way a senior officer does a small favor for a junior officer who would otherwise have to do an awkward thing in front of the wrong audience.

The favor was noted. Not named aloud.

Diego deserves a longer account here, because there is a moment, twenty-eight days from this conversation on the wing, when his decision to surface his disagreement with the consortium routing at the right hour rather than the right meeting is going to matter in a way none of us could have read on Day 2 of the Drake. I have served with him on three boats before this one. He poured me coffee on a Russian icebreaker in the Weddell in 2014. He poured me coffee on a Norwegian polar logistics vessel in 2019. He poured me coffee on the *Otago* in 2022 the morning we lost the starboard transducer to a floe his polar-ops file had warned us about and that the consortium's pre-mission file had not. The transducer cost the mission three days. Diego did not tell me, when we surfaced, that his file had named the floe. He let me find it in the post-mission archive on my own. When I asked him about it on the dock at Lyttelton, he said: *the file was where the file should have been. The decision was at the planning meeting.* He did not blame the planning meeting either. That is the kind of man he is. The kind who has the answer on his chart at fourteen-twenty on Day 2 and brings it to the wing at the watch on which the answer is operationally actionable, and not at the meeting where the answer would have been politically useful.

## The Absence of Obstruction — Hiroshi

On Day 3 the wind dropped. The Drake gave us, as it sometimes gives, a forty-hour window of merely-difficult weather between the worst of the system that had carried us out of the Strait and whatever the next system would do. The boat took advantage of it; the watch officers did the same. The deck plates briefly became surfaces a person could cross without holding the rail, and the wardroom had its first hot meal that did not require a hand to keep the plate where the plate had been set down, and Hiroshi opened his console under conditions that did not require the console's gimbal to do its full work.

At the start of his afternoon watch, Hiroshi was at the data console with two of the chief-scientist daily forms open and a sediment-core deferral list on his secondary screen and a piece of the shore-side correspondence he had been maintaining since the boat left the pier on his tertiary. He nodded at me when I came in. He did not break the angle of his eyes from his screens for longer than the nod required. The question of whether the morning had been productive did not come up. Hiroshi's mornings are productive on the metric Hiroshi runs; his mornings are productive on the metric the consortium runs; if the two ever diverged the consortium's metric would be the one to adjust. Other chief scientists I had sailed with carried anxieties I spent the first week of every cruise managing. Hiroshi does not require management. Hiroshi requires the absence of obstruction.

He said, when I came back through the comm space later and asked whether he needed anything from me before the evening brief: *I have what I need, Director.* He said it without looking up. He said it in the tone that meant the answer was complete. I went forward.

The calmest man on the manifest had set the calmest watch I had seen since the boat left the pier. Hiroshi's calm is contagious in the way other officers' anxiety is contagious. The watch ran on his tempo.

## Attestation Before Partition — Wanjiru

Wanjiru came up to me at the rail on the afternoon of Day 4 with a relay-stack diagnostic on her tablet and a question that was not on the diagnostic. She had been humming when she came up. The hum was the same Kikuyu fragment I had caught at the gangway. The tempo was unchanged.

She said: *Director. Surface ops are clean. The relay stack has been propagating to the consortium ports on the cadence we agreed before the boat left the pier; the gossip protocol is moving every author's writes within budget; the audit-log integrity check ran at oh-eight-hundred and oh-fourteen-hundred without finding anything. The architecture is operating to specification on the surface side. I want you to know I have verified it.*

I said: *acknowledged.*

She said: *the dive will change what the architecture is doing. I want you to attest the surface state before we lose it.*

I said: *I have attested it. The relay layer is healthy. The gossip protocol is current. Consortium ports have the writes through this morning.*

She nodded. The hum resumed. She went below.

Other comm officers would not have thought to install the baseline. The surface-side claim would have been assumed in the way operational baselines are usually assumed, which is to say by reference to the absence of any complaint. Wanjiru did not assume by absence. Wanjiru attested. The attestation was procedure, not theatre. She had done the same thing the architecture would do, in the same kind of sentence the architecture would use, on the surface, before the dive. The bearing ran through both of them.

The architecture's specification has a sentence in it that says: *at the close of a relay window, the surface state is countersigned and replicated to the local store of every node.* Wanjiru, on the wing at the afternoon of Day 4, said the same sentence in the human register. She named the surface state. She asked me to attest it. I attested it. The architecture, two compartments below us, was about to be asked to do the same thing on its own carrier under conditions it had not yet been tested under. The architecture's practice was Wanjiru's practice made portable. I went below and logged it in those words.

## The Fourth Pass — Priya

Day 5 the wind picked up again. The boat re-entered the bearing it had carried out of the Strait, with the deck plates moving and the wardroom plates requiring a hand and the gimbal on Hiroshi's console doing its full work. The watch tightened. The brief tightened. Crew passing each other in the corridors compressed their cadence to that of a boat that had begun to do the work the boat was sent to do.

Sabina was still at her desk. The hardcopies had moved from the manila folder onto the bench in three stacks and had been reordered by one of the consortium-port categories I had not yet asked about. Diego was alternating between the bridge and the polar-operations station two compartments aft of the bridge, where the under-ice routing maps were laid out and where he had begun pre-marking the deviations from the consortium routing he expected the boat to need at the edge. Hiroshi was at the data console. Wanjiru was at the comm space. Maria had moved through the boat at oh-eight-hundred with the morning roster and made the rounds again at thirteen-thirty, and the medical bay had a queue of two minor watch-related injuries for which she had set up an evening clinic. Joel was below, in the engineering compartment, on something that did not require my attention.

That left Priya.

---

Priya was in the instrumentation lab on the lower deck. The lab was the compartment with the calibration hardware racked along the inboard bulkhead, the deployment-rated sensor heads stowed in steel cradles forward, the workstations she had been running her pre-deployment checks on since Day 1, and the bench at the centre of the compartment with the shock-mounted reference fixture she had asked the consortium to ship and had hand-carried up the gangway in her three crates at Punta Arenas.

Fifteen-forty. Priya was at the bench. The reference fixture was on the bench. Two of the deployment sensor heads were in the fixture's cradles. The third was in a steel tray to her right, with a calibration cable attached and the cable's other end at her workstation. The screen had three windows open in the configuration I had seen on every video call we had taken before the mission - pass log, deviation plot, schema-version handshake transcript - and the windows were arranged in the order she runs them, which is the order they catch things in.

She was on the third pass.

She had said it aloud as I came in: *pass three*, in the clipped one-word cadence she uses when she is concentrating. She set the calibration cable down on the bench at the next motion, with the audible weight she sets objects down with, and she ran the cycle.

The boat moved. The bench moved. Its reference fixture took the motion in the way the shock-mounting was designed to take it. Priya's hands moved with controlled efficiency through the cycle. Her shoulders were set. Her jaw was tight. She was counting through the cycle in the way Priya counts, which is partly aloud and partly under her breath and entirely visible if you know what you are watching.

The cycle finished. The deviation plot showed the third pass falling within tolerance on eight of the nine sub-channels and at the high edge of tolerance on the ninth.

Priya looked at the ninth.

She did not say anything.

She set the calibration cable back on the bench, with the same audible weight as before.

She said: *Director.*

I said: *yes.*

*Pass one matched spec. Pass two matched spec. Pass three is at the upper edge of tolerance on sub-channel nine. The deviation is within consortium spec. The deviation is not within mine. I want a fourth pass before the deployment package goes back into the cradle.*

I said: *acknowledged.*

She did not negotiate. She did not offer the operational context for why a fourth pass would or would not be needed. The question of whether the consortium's tolerance was the operative constraint, or her own, went unasked. She had already decided. Her tolerance was the operative constraint; the boat was one of the days that mattered. She had brought it to me as a fact, not a request. I had received it as a fact.

She started the fourth pass.

From the doorway, the first ninety seconds: Priya counted under her breath. The numbers came in clean. The sub-windows on the workstation showed them as she counted. The pass log logged the entry. The deviation plot updated. Sub-channel nine returned to mid-tolerance; the other eight held where they had been on the third pass. The fixture took the boat's motion. Priya took the fixture's motion. Both of them took it.

She acknowledged the watch officer who came in two minutes later with a question about the deployment manifest. She said *yes* without looking up. The follow-up got *no*. *Clear*, on the third exchange. The watch officer accepted the three words and went forward. She didn't come up for him, or for the Mission Director either.

Leaving the lab, the note was this: Priya's fourth pass was not a thing she had announced as a deviation from procedure. It was a thing she had decided was procedure on the boat about to dive under the ice. The consortium's three-pass standard had been the floor; the fourth was the ceiling she ran her own work to. She had not asked the consortium for permission to raise the ceiling. She had not consulted me on whether to raise it. She had raised it. The lift cost the boat about eleven additional minutes of calibration time. The boat could afford them. The architecture, if a sub-channel-nine fault propagated through schema migration under partition six weeks from now, would not have to spend them recovering.

You will hear, in the months that come, a great deal about who failed this mission and who did not. I am going to ask you to keep this fourth pass in your mind when you read it. Priya raised the ceiling on the morning of Day 5 in the Drake, before any of the architecture had been tested under the ice, before the question of whether the architecture would hold had even become a question, because that is what she does. I have known her since she was a postdoc at ETH. She has never asked me to notice the ceiling. I have always noticed.

The fourth pass was logged. The eleven minutes too. I did not name what the pass was costing Priya or what it was buying the architecture. The naming was not for the watch log. The naming is for the staff history — the document where I get to do the naming the watch log does not. You are the reason it exists.

Priya finished. She returned the deployment sensor heads to the steel cradles forward, closed the three sub-windows in the order she had opened them, set her tablet down on the bench with the audible weight, and went to the wardroom for the evening meal she had earned. I saw her at the wardroom hatch as she came up. She nodded. She did not say anything that did not need to be said. I withheld what would have cost her a sentence she had not brought to the meal. The discipline was where it belonged.

## Recorded Fact, Not Memory — Joel

Joel came up from the engineering compartment on the morning of Day 6 with a tablet and a specific question about an under-ice acoustic-only mesh test he wanted to run before the dive. He found me in the comm space with Wanjiru. *Director.* He said it the way he had on the dock: a senior engineer addressing a colleague he had been working with long enough that the title was a register-marker, not a deference.

*I want to run the acoustic-only mesh rehearsal before the dive. Surface conditions, daylight, with the relay layer up so we have the comparison available. The rehearsal exercises the sync layer on the acoustic carrier the way the deployment will exercise it under the ice. If the rehearsal shows what the pre-mission models predicted, we go into the dive with the comparison logged. If it shows something the models did not predict, we have surface comms to triage before we lose them.*

I said: *what does the rehearsal exercise that the surface relay does not?*

*The acoustic carrier has higher latency, lower throughput, and a distinct error profile from the radio relay. The sync protocol's behavior on the carrier is the behavior we will see under the ice. The architecture handles both carriers; the protocol is the same code path; the operational profile is different. The rehearsal lets us see the operational profile while the comparison to the radio relay is still observable. After the dive we will not have that comparison.*

I said: *time required.*

*Forty minutes for the rehearsal. Twenty for pre-rehearsal setup. Ten for post-rehearsal review with Wanjiru and Priya. Total seventy. I would like to run it on the afternoon watch on Day 6, before the marginal zone is on us.*

I said: *authorized. Coordinate with Wanjiru on the relay timing. Brief the watch officer. Brief me on the results before the evening meal.*

He nodded once. *Acknowledged.* He went forward.

What I had taken in was not the rehearsal's technical scope. The scope was correct on its face. What I had taken in was Joel's instinct for the comparison's window. After the dive the comparison was gone. The architecture was going to operate on the acoustic carrier under conditions the pre-mission models had described and the boat had not yet exercised. Joel wanted the boat to exercise the carrier with the radio relay still up, so the comparison was a recorded fact rather than a memory of one. It was the instinct of an engineer who had spent a long career on systems whose behavior under stress had to be a recorded fact, not a remembered one.

You may, if you have read the earlier chapter about the afternoon I interviewed Joel, recognize the discipline I am describing here. It is the same discipline that produced the four sentences on the call. It is the same discipline that produced the rewrite of the lease protocol after the council placed the hold. You are watching it now in its operational register, on a research boat moving into the marginal zone on Day 6 of a fifty-six day mission, in the form of a man asking for seventy minutes of surface time to make a comparison the architecture would otherwise have to live without. I selected Joel because I needed an architect whose response to a partition under load was the kind that produced this rehearsal. The rehearsal was the proof. I did not yet need the proof - the architecture had not yet been tested under the ice; the proof was still ahead of us - but I noted that what I was getting from Joel on Day 6 was what I had bought from him on the call in May.

---

The rehearsal held. Joel briefed me at sixteen-forty and confirmed the comparison was now a recorded fact.

## The Edge — Day 7

The Nansen reached the marginal ice zone at oh-three-forty on Day 7. I was on the bridge cap when we reached it. The bridge officer of the night watch had given me the call at oh-three-twenty; I had come up at oh-three-thirty with the parka I keep at the hatch and a thermos of tea I had brewed at oh-three-fifteen. The wind had dropped again. The light had begun to change. The flat dark of the southern night had begun to show, on the horizon to the south, the band of paler grey that marked the reflection of the marginal zone against the cloud cover.

At the rail, the band lightened. The first brash-ice pieces appeared in the swells off the port quarter — fragments the size of a deck plate, the size of a wardroom plate, the size of a fist — and then larger, and then the first of the floes drifted past at a distance the bridge officer logged on the chart and that Diego, who came up at oh-three-fifty-eight without my having called him, named as the leading edge of the marginal zone proper.

Diego said: *the edge is where I said it was.*

He said it without performance. He said it the way a man states a fact he had brought to me at the wing at fourteen-twenty on Day 2 and that the boat had now arrived at on the schedule he had drawn against the chart. I noted the fact. I said nothing. The bridge officer logged the position. Mikael came up at oh-four-ten and acknowledged the position and the timing and Diego's pre-marking on the polar chart, and he and Diego stood at the wing together for some minutes after that without saying anything that did not need to be said.

The bakery on Liteyny Prospekt came to mind: the dark rye bread my mother and I shared every visit. The bakery is two blocks from the hospital where she now lives.
