The Strait of Magellan was behind us by the second watch of Day 1. The Strait is its own kind of water — sheltered, glacial-fed, the grey-green you can stand at the rail of and read the depth of by eye. The Strait is not the Drake. The boat knew the difference before any of us did. Around eighteen hundred, the Nansen began to take the first of the swells the boat takes on the southern leg, and from that hour on the deck moved.

I have crossed the Drake Passage eleven times. The first crossing was in 2006, on a Russian supply vessel out of Ushuaia, when I was a junior member of a science party rotating to Bellingshausen and back. The boat was old. The crossing was rough. I learned, in the second night out, that *rough* in the Drake's lexicon meant nothing the boat itself had not been built to absorb, and that my own definition of rough — formed in the North Atlantic and on three Baltic crossings — was a definition the Drake did not honour. I have not used the word casually since. The Drake did not behave. The Drake was not built to behave. The boat was built to take what the Drake gave it and continue south. That was the contract.

The Nansen took it. She took it through the night of Day 1 and the morning of Day 2 and the long grey watch that ran from Day 2 noon to Day 3 dawn, and she took it the way a vessel of her class is supposed to take it, which is to say that she pitched and she rolled and she did not, at any hour, sound like a boat in distress. The bridge officer of the morning watch on Day 3 told me the wind at oh-three-hundred had been forty-two knots gusting fifty-six. He told me with the flatness watch officers use for weather they do not intend to characterize. I noted the flatness. I logged the wind. I went below.

---

Sabina was already at the wardroom desk when I came down. She had the consortium's port-call list out on the bench in two formats — a paper printout in the manila folder she carries everywhere and a tablet view in front of her — and she was reconciling the third item on each against a separate sheet of notes she had been keeping since an hour I had not been awake for. Her coffee was at her right hand and was not steaming. She looked up. She said *Director.* She said it in the register Sabina has, which is the register of a person who has been on the work since before the watch began and will remain on the work until the work releases her.

I sat down across from her. She walked me through what the consortium ports would expect from us. The first surface window at Day 21 — Helsinki ground station's relay schedule, signed in by AARI and the Geneva science office, Norwegian Polar Institute as the secondary contact, the Brazilian Antarctic program as the tertiary. The second surface window at Day 39 — the same primary, a different secondary, an additional consortium audit checkpoint the UAE-led leg of the consortium had asked for and that Sabina had pre-cleared with the AARI legal office before the boat had left port. The third window at Day 56, on approach to Punta Arenas — full propagation of the mission archive, the formal post-mission accounting, the medical roster's final state, the consortium's regulatory filings. She had paper copies of the signoffs for all three.

I had asked her once, a year before the mission, why the paper. She had said the network was not where the work lived. She had said it the way one says a thing learned at a specific hour on a specific afternoon at a stage of one's life one does not now revisit. I did not ask again. The paper continued to be made; the paper continued to travel; the paper sat in the folder at her right hand on the morning of Day 2 in the Drake.

She said, midway through the second-window walkthrough: *the consortium will want the audit checkpoint to be clean.* She said it as a statement of operational fact, without commentary on whether the consortium's wanting it was reasonable or whether the UAE-led leg had pre-positioned the request for political reasons an officer who had spent twenty years reconciling consortium signoffs would have spotted. The wanting was the constraint. The cleanness was the work. She did not editorialize.

I have served with logistics officers who editorialized. The editorializing is sometimes useful and is more often a tax the rest of the watch pays for the officer's positioning inside consortium politics. Sabina did not pay that tax. Sabina did the work. I logged it. I returned to the deck.

---

Diego came up to the bridge wing at fourteen-twenty on Day 2 with a thermos he had brought from his cabin and the polar-operations chart he keeps on a folded paper that has been folded and unfolded enough times to have a topography of its own. He set the thermos down on the rail and the chart on the bench under the windscreen. He poured. He gave me half. The coffee was the kind a man drinks when the coffee is for the work and not for the pleasure of it. I have known Diego for fifteen years. He has poured me a cup of that coffee on three previous vessels.

I asked him what the ice edge would look like at this season.

*The edge moves, Anna.* He said it the way a senior operator says a thing to another senior operator. *September is late winter for the southern continent; the edge has not retreated yet. We will reach it sooner than the consortium routing assumes. We will need to slow before the boat's master assumes we should.*

He tapped the chart at a point that was not where the consortium's pre-mission file had marked the expected ice edge. *The routing was drawn in Geneva. Geneva does not see the edge from the boat's wing. I have seen the edge from the boat's wing on this latitude in five Septembers. The edge is at this line, give or take a watch, depending on what the wind does.*

I asked him about the under-ice acoustic conditions on approach.

He paused. The pause was the pause of a man whose answer was a list and who was deciding which items belonged in the answer he gave the Mission Director on the bridge wing on Day 2 and which belonged in the longer briefing he and I would both prefer at a later hour. *The marginal ice zone is loud. Open water under broken ice is louder than open water without ice; the brash-ice pieces grind against each other; the larger floes work against the swell underneath them. The acoustic floor on approach will not be clean. The boat's sonar will read the zone correctly because the sonar was designed to read it; the data instrumentation will pick up signal floor the consortium's pre-mission noise budget did not model in full. Priya will need to recalibrate before deployment. We can budget the time.*

He drank his coffee. He folded the chart back into its topography. He carried the thermos below.

What I had registered, in Diego's two answers, was not the technical content. The content was correct; I would have been surprised if it had not been. What I had registered was the shape. Diego had a list of things that were going to be different at the ice edge from what the consortium had assumed, and he had been carrying the list at low altitude for the duration of the planning, and he had not surfaced it in the planning meetings because the meetings were not where the list belonged. The bridge wing on Day 2 was where the list belonged. He had brought it on schedule.

I have served with polar specialists whose institutional rank exceeded Diego's and whose performance-of-expertise exceeded his by an order of magnitude. None of them had been quietly correct on the bridge wing the way Diego had been quietly correct on the bridge wing. Diego does not perform competence. Diego does the work. The performing is for the consortium meetings. The work is for the hours between them.

Before he went below he said the boat's master had registered the same difference between the consortium routing and the actual edge but had not yet decided how to bring it up to the Mission Director. Diego was bringing it up first. He said it the way a senior officer does a small favor for a junior officer who would otherwise have to do an awkward thing in front of the wrong audience.

I noted the favor. I did not name it aloud.

---

On Day 3 the wind dropped. The Drake gave us, as the Drake sometimes gives, a forty-hour window of merely-difficult weather between the worst of the system that had carried us out of the Strait and whatever the next system would do. The boat took advantage of it. The watch officers took advantage of it. The deck plates briefly became surfaces a person could cross without holding the rail, and the wardroom had its first hot meal that did not require a hand to keep the plate where the plate had been set down, and Hiroshi opened his console under conditions that did not require the console's gimbal to do its full work.

I checked on Hiroshi at the start of his afternoon watch. He was at the data console with two of the chief-scientist daily forms open and a sediment-core deferral list on his secondary screen and a piece of the shore-side correspondence he had been maintaining since the boat left the pier on his tertiary. He nodded at me when I came in. He did not break the angle of his eyes from his screens for longer than the nod required. I did not ask him whether the morning had been productive. Hiroshi's mornings are productive on the metric Hiroshi runs; his mornings are productive on the metric the consortium runs; if the two ever diverged the consortium's metric would be the one to adjust. I have served with chief scientists whose anxiety I have spent the first week of every cruise managing. Hiroshi does not require management. Hiroshi requires the absence of obstruction.

He said, when I came back through the comm space later and asked whether he needed anything from me before the evening brief: *I have what I need, Director.* He said it without looking up. He said it in the register that meant the answer was complete. I went forward.

The calmest man on the manifest had set the calmest watch I had registered since the boat left the pier. Hiroshi's calm is contagious in the way other officers' anxiety is contagious. The watch ran on his tempo.

---

Wanjiru came up to me at the rail on the afternoon of Day 4 with a relay-stack diagnostic on her tablet and a question that was not on the diagnostic. She had been humming when she came up. The hum was the same Kikuyu fragment I had registered at the gangway. The tempo was unchanged.

She said: *Director. Surface ops are clean. The relay stack has been propagating to the consortium ports on the cadence we agreed before the boat left the pier; the gossip protocol is moving every author's writes within budget; the audit-log integrity check ran at oh-eight-hundred and oh-fourteen-hundred without finding anything. The architecture is operating to specification on the surface side. I want you to know I have verified it.*

I said: *acknowledged.*

She said: *the dive will change what the architecture is doing. I want you to register the surface state before we lose it.*

I said: *I have registered it. The relay layer is healthy. The gossip protocol is current. The consortium ports have the writes through this morning.*

She nodded. The hum resumed. She went below.

What the exchange had been, I logged afterward at the wardroom desk, was Wanjiru installing a baseline. The baseline was an operational fact: the relay layer operates correctly when the network is healthy. The fact had not been at issue. The fact was the floor on which every other architectural property the mission was about to test would stand. Wanjiru wanted that floor named, on the watch log, before it was no longer reachable.

I have served with comm officers who would not have thought to install the baseline. The surface-side claim would have been assumed in the way operational baselines are usually assumed, which is to say by reference to the absence of any complaint. Wanjiru did not assume by absence. Wanjiru attested. The attestation was procedure, not theatre. She had done the same thing the architecture would do, in the same kind of sentence the architecture would use, on the surface, before the dive. The discipline ran through both of them.

---

Day 5 the wind picked up again. The boat re-entered the register the boat had carried out of the Strait, with the deck plates moving and the wardroom plates requiring a hand and the gimbal on Hiroshi's console doing its full work. The watch tightened. The brief tightened. The tempo at which crew passed each other in the corridors compressed to the tempo of crew on a boat that has begun to do the work the boat was sent to do.

Sabina was still at the wardroom desk. The paper had moved from the manila folder onto the bench in three stacks and had been reordered by one of the consortium-port categories I had not yet asked about. Diego was alternating between the bridge and the polar-operations station two compartments aft of the bridge, where the under-ice routing maps were laid out and where he had begun pre-marking the deviations from the consortium routing he expected the boat to need at the marginal ice zone. Hiroshi was at the data console. Wanjiru was at the comm space. Maria had moved through the boat at oh-eight-hundred with the morning roster and made the rounds again at thirteen-thirty, and the medical bay had a queue of two minor watch-related injuries for which she had set up an evening clinic. Joel was below, in the engineering compartment, on something that did not require my attention.

That left Priya.

---

Priya was in the instrumentation lab on the lower deck. The lab was the compartment with the calibration hardware racked along the inboard bulkhead, the deployment-rated sensor heads stowed in steel cradles forward, the workstations she had been running her pre-deployment checks on since Day 1, and the bench at the centre of the compartment with the shock-mounted reference fixture she had asked the consortium to ship and had hand-carried up the gangway in her three crates at Punta Arenas.

I came down at fifteen-forty. Priya was at the bench. The reference fixture was on the bench. Two of the deployment sensor heads were in the fixture's cradles. The third was in a steel tray to her right, with a calibration cable attached and the cable's other end at her workstation. The screen had three windows open in the configuration I had registered on every video call we had taken before the mission — pass log, deviation plot, schema-version handshake transcript — and the windows were arranged in the order she runs them, which is the order they catch things in.

She was on the third pass.

I knew because she had said it aloud as I came in. She said *pass three* in the clipped one-word register she uses when she is concentrating, and she set the calibration cable down on the bench at the next motion, with the audible weight she sets objects down with, and she ran the cycle.

The boat moved. The bench moved. The reference fixture took the motion in the way the fixture's shock-mounting was designed to take it. Priya's hands moved with controlled efficiency through the cycle. Her shoulders were set. Her jaw was tight. She was counting through the cycle in the way Priya counts, which is partly aloud and partly under her breath and entirely visible if you know what you are watching.

The cycle finished. The deviation plot showed the third pass falling within tolerance on eight of the nine sub-channels and at the high edge of tolerance on the ninth.

Priya looked at the ninth.

She did not say anything.

She set the calibration cable back on the bench, with the same audible weight as before.

She said: *Director.*

I said: *yes.*

*Pass one matched spec. Pass two matched spec. Pass three is at the upper edge of tolerance on sub-channel nine. The deviation is within consortium spec. The deviation is not within mine. I want a fourth pass before the deployment package goes back into the cradle.*

I said: *acknowledged.*

She did not negotiate. She did not offer the operational context for why a fourth pass would or would not be needed. She did not ask whether the consortium's tolerance was the operative constraint or whether her own tolerance was. She had already decided. The decision was that her tolerance was the operative constraint. The decision was that the boat was one of the days that mattered. She had brought it to me as a fact, not a request. I had received it as a fact.

She started the fourth pass.

I watched the first ninety seconds from the doorway. Priya counted under her breath. The numbers were the numbers. The sub-windows on the workstation registered the numbers as she counted. The pass log registered the entry. The deviation plot updated. Sub-channel nine returned to mid-tolerance on the fourth pass. The other eight remained where they had been on the third. The fixture took the boat's motion. Priya took the fixture's motion. The discipline took both.

She acknowledged the watch officer who came in two minutes later with a question about the deployment manifest. She said *yes* without looking up. She said *no* to a follow-up. She said *clear* on the third exchange. The watch officer accepted the three words and went forward. The discipline did not lift for the watch officer. The discipline did not lift for the Mission Director either. The discipline was the work.

I noted, leaving the lab, that Priya's fourth pass was not a thing she had announced as a deviation from procedure. The fourth pass was a thing she had decided was procedure on the boat that was about to dive under the ice. The consortium's three-pass standard had been the floor; the fourth pass was the ceiling she ran her own work to. She had not asked the consortium for permission to raise the ceiling. She had not consulted me on whether to raise it. She had raised it. The raising had cost the boat about eleven additional minutes of calibration time. The eleven minutes were eleven minutes the boat could afford. The fourth pass was eleven minutes the architecture, if a sub-channel-nine fault propagated through schema migration under partition six weeks from now, would not have to spend recovering.

I logged the fourth pass. I logged the eleven minutes. I did not name what the fourth pass was costing Priya or what the fourth pass was buying the architecture. The naming was not for the watch log. The naming is for the staff history, which is what I am writing now.

Priya finished the fourth pass. She returned the deployment sensor heads to the steel cradles forward. She closed the three sub-windows in the order she had opened them. She set her tablet down on the bench, with the audible weight, and she went to the wardroom for the evening meal she had earned. I saw her at the wardroom hatch as she came up. She nodded. She did not say anything that did not need to be said. I did not say anything she would have had to receive at the cost of a sentence she had not brought to the meal. The fourth pass was logged. The eleven minutes were logged. The discipline was where it belonged.

---

Joel came up from the engineering compartment on the morning of Day 6 with a tablet and a specific question about an under-ice acoustic-only mesh test he wanted to run before the dive. He found me in the comm space with Wanjiru. *Director.* He said it the way he had on the dock: a senior engineer addressing a colleague he had been working with long enough that the title was a register-marker, not a deference.

*I want to run the acoustic-only mesh rehearsal before the dive. Surface conditions, daylight, with the relay layer up so we have the comparison available. The rehearsal exercises the sync layer on the acoustic carrier the way the deployment will exercise it under the ice. If the rehearsal shows what the pre-mission models predicted, we go into the dive with the comparison logged. If it shows something the models did not predict, we have surface comms to triage before we lose them.*

I said: *what does the rehearsal exercise that the surface relay does not?*

*The acoustic carrier has higher latency, lower throughput, and a distinct error profile from the radio relay. The sync protocol's behavior on the carrier is the behavior we will see under the ice. The architecture handles both carriers; the protocol is the same code path; the operational profile is different. The rehearsal lets us see the operational profile while the comparison to the radio relay is still observable. After the dive we will not have that comparison.*

I said: *time required.*

*Forty minutes for the rehearsal. Twenty minutes of pre-rehearsal setup. Ten minutes of post-rehearsal review with Wanjiru and Priya. Total seventy. I would like to run it on the afternoon watch on Day 6, before the marginal ice zone is on us.*

I said: *authorized. Coordinate with Wanjiru on the relay timing. Brief the watch officer. Brief me on the results before the evening meal.*

He nodded once. *Acknowledged.* He went forward.

What I had registered was not the rehearsal's technical scope. The scope was the scope. What I had registered was Joel's instinct for the comparison's window. After the dive the comparison was gone. The architecture was going to operate on the acoustic carrier under conditions the pre-mission models had described and the boat had not yet exercised. Joel wanted the boat to exercise the carrier with the radio relay still up, so the comparison was a recorded fact rather than a memory of one. The instinct was the instinct of an engineer who had spent a long career on systems whose behavior under stress had to be a recorded fact, not a remembered one.

---

The rehearsal ran on the afternoon watch on Day 6 between fourteen-twenty and fifteen-thirty. The acoustic-only mesh held. The sync protocol's behavior matched the pre-mission models within the expected envelope on every metric Joel and Wanjiru had instrumented for. Priya brought her instrumentation calibration into the mesh on the rehearsal's second pass and the sub-channels she had recalibrated on Day 5 returned within tolerance under the carrier's error profile. Joel briefed me at sixteen-forty. He said the rehearsal had matched the specification. He said the dive on Day 7 could proceed against the architecture's pre-mission profile. He said the comparison was now a recorded fact.

I said: *acknowledged.* He went below.

---

The Nansen reached the marginal ice zone at oh-three-forty on Day 7. I was on the bridge wing when we reached it. The bridge officer of the night watch had given me the call at oh-three-twenty; I had come up at oh-three-thirty with the parka I keep at the hatch and a thermos of tea I had brewed at oh-three-fifteen. The wind had dropped again. The light had begun to change. The flat dark of the southern night had begun to show, on the horizon to the south, the band of paler grey that marked the reflection of the marginal zone against the cloud cover.

I stood at the rail. I watched the band lighten. I watched the first of the brash-ice pieces appear in the swells off the boat's port quarter. The brash-ice was small at first — fragments the size of a deck plate, the size of a wardroom plate, the size of a fist — and then larger, and then the first of the floes drifted past at a distance the bridge officer registered on the chart and that Diego, who came up at oh-three-fifty-eight without my having called him, registered as the leading edge of the marginal zone proper.

Diego said: *the edge is where I said it was.*

He said it without performance. He said it the way a man states a fact he had brought to me on the bridge wing at fourteen-twenty on Day 2 and that the boat had now arrived at on the schedule he had drawn against the chart. I noted the fact. I did not say anything. The bridge officer logged the position. The boat's master came up at oh-four-ten and acknowledged the position and the timing and Diego's pre-marking on the polar chart, and the master and Diego stood at the wing together for some minutes after that without saying anything that did not need to be said.

I thought, for a moment, of the bakery on Liteyny Prospekt and the dark rye bread my mother and I shared every visit, and the thought was a single sentence and I did not stay in it. I returned to the wing. The tea in the thermos was still warm. I drank what was left.

The boat came up to the rendezvous point with the consortium's deployment marker at oh-six-forty. The instrumentation deployment package was checked once more in the lab — Priya at the bench; the four-pass calibration log on her workstation; the deployment sensor heads back in the steel cradles forward; the schema-version handshake transcript clean — and was moved to the deployment hatch on the lower deck at oh-seven-fifteen. Joel ran the final under-ice rehearsal sequence on the acoustic-only mesh between oh-seven-twenty and oh-seven-fifty. Wanjiru ran the final relay-layer attestation at oh-seven-fifty-five and confirmed the surface state for the watch log: *the relay layer is operating correctly; the gossip protocol is current; the consortium ports have the writes through this morning.* I logged the attestation. I countersigned it. The signature attached to the local store and replicated across the relevant nodes on the boat.

I went to the bridge at oh-eight-hundred. The boat's master was at the wheel. The deck officer was at the dive station. The line officers were at the trim controls. The bridge log was open at the navigation station with the dive entry pre-formatted in the boat's master's hand. The consortium's pre-mission file was on the secondary display, at the page I had been at since five in the morning, with the dive checklist completed in seven of seven fields.

I gave the master the nod I had given seventeen masters at seventeen sailings.

The master acknowledged. The deck officer signalled the dive station. The boat's pumps began the cycle. The Nansen began to settle.

I looked once at the band of grey-green water off the port quarter, at the brash-ice in the swell, at the grey light of the southern dawn the marginal zone had brought with it. I noted what the daylight looked like at the surface on the morning of Day 7. I noted what the ice edge looked like from the bridge wing of a research vessel that was about to leave the surface for fourteen days. I noted what the boat felt like under my feet at the moment the architecture I was about to take three hundred meters under the ice was operating, on the surface side, to its specification.

The architecture was operating. The crew was where the crew should be. The boat was where the boat should be. Sabina was at the wardroom desk; Diego had moved from the wing to the polar-operations station two compartments aft; Hiroshi was at the data console; Wanjiru was at the comm space; Priya was at the lab with the four-pass calibration logged and the deployment ready; Maria was at the medical bay with the dive checklist for the crew; Joel was at the engineering compartment with the boat's chief engineer.

The Nansen settled.

I did not look back. I stood at the bridge for the seven minutes the dive sequence required and I logged the bridge readings as they came in, and at oh-eight-thirty-seven the boat passed below the surface for the first time on the mission, and the southern continent was no longer a place I could see from the bridge wing. The continent was the work. The work was what I had come for.

I did not name what I expected the next days to hold. I had not come to name them. The dive was the next thing.

I went below.
